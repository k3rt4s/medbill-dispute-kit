# Rule 00 — First principles

These three facts about US medical billing precede every tactic in this kit. Every other rule is a special case of one of these.

## Principle 1 — Prices vary enormously and have no relationship to cost or quality

A knee replacement can cost $20,000 at one facility and $80,000 at another in the same city for the same outcome. A comprehensive metabolic panel can cost $15 at an independent lab and $995 at a hospital. A walking boot can cost $50 on Amazon and $335 from a hospital's bedside medical-device vendor.

The implication: the price on the bill is not "the price." It is a number the provider chose, and it does not bind you. Every dispute starts from this fact.

## Principle 2 — Everything is negotiable

Hospital "chargemaster" prices are starting points for insurance negotiations. The same hospital will accept very different amounts from Medicare, from Blue Cross, from United, and from a cash patient who pushes back. Once you know this, the question is not "can I get a discount?" but "what is the right number to offer?"

Many providers maintain documented cash prices that are lower than what insured patients are charged. Asking is free.

## Principle 3 — None of this is required to make sense

Patients assume there's a rational pricing system underneath. There isn't. A given line item may be billed twice for no reason. A 15-minute ER visit may be coded at the highest severity level. An "operating room" charge may appear on a bill for a bedside procedure. Charges may bear no relationship to the contractually agreed rates.

The implication: assume errors. Most medical bills contain at least one. Most contain several. Verifying every line item is the patient's job because nobody else has the incentive to do it.

## What this kit does with these principles

Every rule that follows is mechanical, not philosophical. You don't have to be confrontational or angry. You have to be patient, paper-trailed, and willing to ask for line items, citations, and supervisors. The system bets you won't. The leverage is that small-claims courts, state insurance regulators, and federal CMS are exactly the venues in which the system loses when called to account, and providers know this.
