# Rule 06 — Use small claims court as leverage, then as a venue

Small claims court is the single biggest piece of leverage a patient has against a provider or insurer. The threat is usually enough; the filing usually settles before hearing; the hearing is winnable for prepared self-represented plaintiffs.

## Why this works asymmetrically

A small claims case usually involves $100-$25,000 (varies by state). For the patient, the case is free to file (~$50-$250 filing fee) and free to argue (no attorney required). For the provider, defending the case costs hundreds of dollars per hour in outside legal fees, paralegal time, and a representative's time at hearing. The economics make even a $500 dispute uneconomical to defend.

Providers know this. The mention of small claims, in writing, with a 30-day deadline, settles a large fraction of disputes that previous letters did not.

## The escalation ladder

1. Initial dispute letter (Rule 02-05). 15-business-day response window.
2. If no response, follow-up phone call and second letter, citing the prior letter.
3. **30-day warning letter** (`templates/letter_30day_warning.md`). This is the formal pre-litigation notice. CC the state insurance department, attorney general's consumer protection division, and the BBB. State the disputed amount, the legal basis, the 30-day deadline, and that you will file in small claims court without further notice if not resolved.
4. Filing.
5. Hearing, if not settled before then.

Each step has a higher cost to the provider than the previous one. Most settlements happen at step 3.

## State-level details

Small claims jurisdictional limits and court names vary. Common examples:

- **Tennessee**: General Sessions Court. Limit $25,000. Filing fee $120-$250 depending on county. No attorney required. No jury trial.
- **California**: Small Claims Division of Superior Court. Limit $12,500 for individuals (some courts higher for COVID-related claims). Attorneys not allowed at the initial hearing.
- **Texas**: Justice Court. Limit $20,000. Attorneys allowed but not required.
- **New York**: City court or town court, small claims part. Limit $5,000 (city) / $3,000 (town/village).
- **Florida**: County Court small claims. Limit $8,000.

For other states, search "[your state] small claims court limit" or check the state's judicial branch website. The state-law worksheet in `references/laws_state_template.md` includes the small-claims line.

## Statute of limitations

Medical billing disputes are typically breach-of-contract claims. The breach-of-contract statute of limitations is usually 3-6 years, depending on the state. Don't wait close to the deadline; the paper trail you need is stronger when the events are recent.

## What you need to bring to court

- The original bill
- The itemized bill (if you obtained one)
- The Explanation of Benefits from your insurance, if applicable
- Every letter you sent (with certified-mail receipts) and every response received
- Evidence of the fair-market price (Turquoise screenshots, Healthcare Bluebook printouts, Medicare rates, retail-equivalent screenshots — with dates)
- A printed copy of the relevant CPT documentation requirements if coding is at issue
- A printed copy of the relevant statutes (state itemization law, No Surprises Act sections, UCC § 2-305 if applicable)
- A one-page chronology of events
- A statement of what you're asking for (refund of $X, dismissal of $X balance, etc.)

Make three copies of every exhibit: one for the judge, one for the defendant, one for you. Label exhibits A, B, C, etc.

## How to argue it

Plain language. Chronological order. State the legal basis once, then prove the facts:

> "Your Honor, I went to [facility] on [date] for [reason]. The bill they sent me contained [specific problem: a charge for a service I didn't receive / an upcoded ER visit / a price ten times the local fair-market rate]. Exhibit A is the itemized bill. Exhibit B is [evidence of the problem]. I asked them to correct it on [date 1], on [date 2], and on [date 3] — Exhibits C, D, E. They have not corrected it. I'm asking the court to order the disputed amount of $X removed from my account, plus my filing fee."

Don't lead with emotion. Lead with documents.

## Threat language for letters

Use this in the 30-day warning, not the initial dispute. The initial dispute is polite. The 30-day warning is formal:

> "If this matter is not resolved within thirty (30) days of the date of this letter, I will file a claim in [State] [Small Claims Court Name] seeking recovery of the disputed amount ($X), court filing fees, service-of-process costs, and any additional remedies permitted by law. I have copied the [State] Department of [Commerce and Insurance / Insurance / Health], the Attorney General's Consumer Protection Division, and the Better Business Bureau on this letter."

## When this rule does not apply

- The disputed amount exceeds your state's small-claims limit. You'd be in the regular civil court system, which probably means hiring counsel. Reconsider whether you can split the claim or settle.
- The opposing party is the federal government or a federal program. Different procedural rules apply.
- You suspect fraud or unauthorized practice of medicine. That's a criminal-referral issue, not a small claims one.
