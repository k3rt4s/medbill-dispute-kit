# Rule 08 — Avoid care you don't need (the cheapest dispute)

The bill you don't receive is the one you don't have to dispute. Roughly 25% of US healthcare spending is on services that aren't medically necessary. Patient-side questioning at the point of care reduces this.

## The rule

Before any non-emergency test, procedure, or new prescription, ask your clinician:

1. "What happens if we wait?"
2. "Is there a less invasive or less expensive option that's equally appropriate?"
3. "How will this test or procedure change what we do next?"

If "wait" is medically reasonable, or if a less-invasive option exists and is equally appropriate, or if the result wouldn't change management — consider declining the service.

## Why this question works

It reframes the clinical conversation from "the risk of doing nothing" (which the clinician naturally emphasizes) to "the risk of doing this" (which is real and often underweighted). Every procedure carries risk. Every test carries the risk of incidental findings that lead to further procedures. The default in US medicine is to do more; the patient is the one person in the room who can ask whether less is appropriate.

Most care is not emergency care. You almost always have time to ask, time to get a second opinion, and time to look up evidence.

## When to specifically push back

- **Imaging "just to be safe"** when no specific finding is being investigated. The yield of low-pretest-probability imaging is low and incidental findings drive cascades of further testing.
- **Routine pre-operative testing** in healthy patients for low-risk procedures. Multiple specialty-society guidelines (Choosing Wisely) advise against this.
- **Antibiotic prescriptions for viral illnesses**. Doesn't treat the illness; risks side effects.
- **Recommendations for elective surgery without exploring non-surgical options first.** Back surgery and joint replacements are the canonical examples; physical therapy, weight loss, injections, and time often produce equivalent outcomes.
- **Annual physicals for healthy adults under 40.** The evidence base is weaker than the cultural expectation.

## Get a second opinion when

- The recommended treatment is invasive or expensive
- The diagnosis is uncertain
- The clinician seems hurried, unfamiliar with your specific condition, or dismissive of your questions
- You've been recommended for surgery that has a non-surgical alternative

The second opinion should be from a clinician who has no financial relationship with the first. Many insurance plans cover second opinions explicitly.

## Choosing Wisely

The American Board of Internal Medicine Foundation maintains a public list of specialty-society "don't do this" recommendations called **Choosing Wisely** (choosingwisely.org). Each specialty has flagged 5-10 commonly-done things that the evidence says shouldn't be routine. This is the authoritative source for "is this service actually evidence-based for my situation?"

## What this doesn't mean

- Don't skip emergency care.
- Don't skip screening recommended by your physician based on your specific risk factors (the question is whether routine screening for asymptomatic adults is appropriate; the answer for many cancers and conditions is "no, not at every age, not every year" — but your situation may differ).
- Don't refuse a treatment your doctor recommends just because it's expensive. Discuss whether it's necessary, then decide.

The rule is to ask the question, not to refuse care.
