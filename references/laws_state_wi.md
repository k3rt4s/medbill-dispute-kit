# Wisconsin state pack

The fully-worked state-law layer for Wisconsin patients. The LLM uses this when the patient's state is Wisconsin. Georgia equivalent at [`laws_state_ga.md`](laws_state_ga.md). All citations verified against public sources as of 2026-05-18. Re-verify annually.

Two things make Wisconsin's patient-side leverage distinctive:

1. **First-party bad faith is a recognized common-law tort** under *Anderson v. Continental Insurance Co.*, 85 Wis. 2d 675 (1978), with **punitive damages available** on a showing of intentional or reckless disregard. This is the single highest-value Wisconsin-specific lever where a fully-insured carrier denies or delays a clearly-covered claim.
2. **Wisconsin's Deceptive Trade Practices Act, Wis. Stat. § 100.18, carries a private right of action** with mandatory recovery of actual pecuniary loss plus **reasonable attorney's fees and costs**, reaching the hospital itself for misleading billing representations.

Wisconsin does **not** have a state surprise-billing act or a state ground-ambulance protection, and § 146.83 covers clinical records rather than billing records. Adjust expectations accordingly.

## Hospital itemization right

- **Statute:** **Wis. Stat. § 146.83** governs access to "patient health care records," which are clinical records prepared by or under the supervision of a health care provider. **It does not by its own terms cover billing records.** Hospital billing-detail rights derive instead from administrative-code patient-rights provisions and from general consumer-protection law.
- **Hospital patient-rights duty:** **Wis. Admin. Code § DHS 124.06(1)(m)** (acute-care hospitals) and the parallel critical-access-hospital provisions require that "The patient shall be permitted to examine, and to receive an explanation of, any bill that the patient receives." See [docs.legis.wisconsin.gov/code/admin_code/dhs/110/124](https://docs.legis.wisconsin.gov/code/admin_code/dhs/110/124).
- **Source for § 146.83:** [docs.legis.wisconsin.gov/document/statutes/146.83](https://docs.legis.wisconsin.gov/document/statutes/146.83); [law.justia.com/codes/wisconsin/chapter-146/section-146-83](https://law.justia.com/codes/wisconsin/chapter-146/section-146-83/).
- **What § 146.83 does require, even though billing-adjacent:**
  - Patient (or authorized representative) may **inspect** clinical records on a signed statement of informed consent — **no inspection fee.**
  - On a request for **copies**, a paper-copy fee schedule applies, $1.00/page for the first 25 pages, declining to $0.30/page above 100. **No fee is permitted for records produced in electronic format**, per *Banuelos v. Univ. of Wis. Hosps. & Clinics Auth.*, 2023 WI 25 (Wisconsin Supreme Court).
  - 30 working days to produce, longer if records are located off-site.
- **Itemized-bill request as a practical matter:** No standalone Wisconsin statute mandates an itemized hospital bill on request the way Georgia's § 10-1-393(b)(14) does. The dispute letter should rely on (a) the DHS 124.06(1)(m) patient-rights duty, (b) Wis. Stat. § 100.18 (misleading billing representations are actionable), and (c) the hospital's own internal billing policy. AB 1014 (2017-2018) would have created an explicit itemized-bill statute but was never enacted.
- **ERISA:** Not preempted — these are provider-side obligations.

## Wisconsin Deceptive Trade Practices Act (DTPA)

- **Statute:** **Wis. Stat. § 100.18** — "Fraudulent representations"
- **Source:** [docs.legis.wisconsin.gov/statutes/statutes/100/18](https://docs.legis.wisconsin.gov/statutes/statutes/100/18); [law.justia.com/codes/wisconsin/chapter-100/section-100-18](https://law.justia.com/codes/wisconsin/chapter-100/section-100-18/)
- **Substance:** Prohibits making to the public any "assertion, representation or statement of fact" that is "untrue, deceptive or misleading," with the intent to induce a transaction. Applies to advertising and to representations made one-on-one to a consumer. Three elements: (1) representation to "the public," which can include a single individual, (2) representation was untrue/deceptive/misleading, (3) the representation caused pecuniary loss.
- **Private right of action:** **Yes**, under § 100.18(11)(b)2. A person who suffers pecuniary loss "shall recover such pecuniary loss, together with costs, including reasonable attorney fees." Doubling applies for violations of a § 100.18 injunction. Fee recovery is **mandatory** on a successful claim, not discretionary.
- **Procedural note:** No pre-suit demand requirement. The kit's 30-day warning letter is not a § 100.18 prerequisite, but it preserves leverage and creates evidence.
- **Statute of limitations:** **3 years** from the unlawful act (§ 100.18(11)(b)3). Shorter than the contract limitations clock, so file or send the demand promptly.
- **Use against hospitals:** Cite for misrepresented amounts owed, misrepresented network/coverage status at point of service, upcoding presented to the patient as accurate, or any billing statement asserting an amount the hospital knew was inflated. UDAP remedy is "moderate" by national standards — not as strong as Massachusetts Ch. 93A or Georgia's FBPA treble damages, but the mandatory attorney-fee shift is the operative lever.

## Unfair Claim Settlement Practices

- **Regulation:** **Wis. Admin. Code § Ins 6.11** — "Insurance claim settlement practices" (Office of the Commissioner of Insurance)
- **Source:** [docs.legis.wisconsin.gov/document/administrativecode/Ins%206.11](https://docs.legis.wisconsin.gov/document/administrativecode/Ins%206.11); [law.cornell.edu/regulations/wisconsin/Wis-Admin-Code-SS-Ins-6-11](https://www.law.cornell.edu/regulations/wisconsin/Wis-Admin-Code-SS-Ins-6-11)
- **Substance:** Defines unfair claim-settlement practices in two categories:
  - **§ Ins 6.11(3)(a)** — practices that constitute unfair settlement only if done **without just cause and as a general business practice**: failure to acknowledge claims promptly, failure to investigate with reasonable dispatch, failure to provide claim forms and assistance, failure to attempt fair settlement where liability is reasonably clear, failure to explain denials on request.
  - **§ Ins 6.11(3)(b)** — practices that are unfair **regardless of frequency**: knowingly misrepresenting policy provisions, failing to maintain adequate claims staffing, failing to adopt reasonable investigation standards.
- **Critical caveat:** **No private right of action.** Wisconsin treats § Ins 6.11 as a regulatory standard enforced by the OCI only. Violations are **admissible as evidence of bad faith** in an Anderson tort claim, but cannot themselves be pleaded as a standalone civil count. Treat exactly the way Georgia's UCSPA is treated.
- **Use:** Cite Ins 6.11 violations in the OCI complaint and as evidentiary support in any *Anderson* bad-faith pleading. Do not plead Ins 6.11 as a standalone cause of action.

## Bad-faith failure to pay — Anderson v. Continental

- **Authority:** ***Anderson v. Continental Insurance Co.***, **85 Wis. 2d 675, 271 N.W.2d 368 (1978)** — Wisconsin Supreme Court
- **Source:** [law.justia.com/cases/wisconsin/supreme-court/1978/76-242-7.html](https://law.justia.com/cases/wisconsin/supreme-court/1978/76-242-7.html); [courtlistener.com/opinion/2063523/anderson-v-continental-ins-co](https://www.courtlistener.com/opinion/2063523/anderson-v-continental-ins-co/); pattern jury instruction at **WIS JI-Civil 2761**, [wilawlibrary.gov/jury/files/civil/2761.pdf](https://wilawlibrary.gov/jury/files/civil/2761.pdf)
- **Substance:** Wisconsin recognizes a **common-law first-party bad-faith tort**, separate from the underlying contract. The tort lies in the breach of an implied duty of good faith and fair dealing that arises from the insurance relationship.
- **Elements (the Anderson two-part test):**
  1. **Absence of a reasonable basis** for denying or delaying benefits under the policy, and
  2. The insurer's **knowledge or reckless disregard** of the lack of a reasonable basis.
- **Damages:**
  - Contract damages for the unpaid benefit,
  - **Consequential / extra-contractual damages** (emotional distress, financial loss, attorney's fees incurred to compel payment),
  - **Punitive damages**, available on the *Anderson* standard of "evil intent... or special ill-will or wanton disregard of duty" (per *Brown v. Maxey*; refined further in *Strenke v. Hogner*, 2005 WI 25). Wis. Stat. § 895.043 caps punitives at the greater of $200,000 or twice compensatory damages.
- **No specific pre-suit demand statute** for bad faith, in contrast to Georgia's § 33-4-6 60-day demand. The kit's standard 30-day warning letter still serves to (a) establish the absence-of-reasonable-basis element by inviting a reasoned response, and (b) document the insurer's state of knowledge.
- **ERISA preemption:** *Anderson* bad-faith claims are **preempted** as to self-funded ERISA employer plans under 29 U.S.C. § 1144(a). Federal remedy in that case is 29 U.S.C. § 1132(a)(1)(B) for benefits plus possible § 1132(g) fee-shifting — no punitives. The tort is **fully in play** for fully-insured plans, individual/marketplace plans, Medicaid managed-care plans, and non-ERISA short-term/limited plans.
- **Standard:** Negligence and good-faith mistake are not enough. The standard is "absence of a reasonable basis" plus knowledge/reckless disregard — substantively similar to Georgia's "frivolous and unfounded" standard, but Wisconsin opens the door to punitives that Georgia § 33-4-6 does not.

## Surprise billing

**Wisconsin has no state surprise-billing act.** Patients in Wisconsin rely on the **federal No Surprises Act (NSA)**, 42 U.S.C. §§ 300gg-111 to 300gg-139, effective January 1, 2022. The OCI is the state-side enforcement partner for fully-insured plans.

- **OCI consumer page:** [oci.wi.gov/Pages/Consumers/NoSurprisesAct.aspx](https://oci.wi.gov/Pages/Consumers/NoSurprisesAct.aspx)
- **Federal help desk:** **1-800-985-3059**
- **Coverage:** OON emergency services, OON ancillary services at an in-network facility, air ambulance.
- **The biggest gap, ground ambulance, is NOT covered** by the NSA. Wisconsin has not closed it. **2025 Senate Bill 711** ("emergency ambulance services coverage and reimbursement") was introduced December 2, 2025 and is pending in committee as of this writing. Until SB 711 or a successor is enacted, a Wisconsin patient receiving an OON ground-ambulance bill has **no state surprise-billing protection** and must rely on Anderson bad faith (if the insurer is the obstacle), § 100.18 (if the ambulance provider's billing is misleading), and direct negotiation.

## Small claims court — Circuit Court small claims

- **Court name:** **Circuit Court, Small Claims branch** (every Wisconsin county; small claims is a procedure within the Circuit Court, not a separate court)
- **Jurisdictional limit:** **$10,000** for contract / money-judgment actions, codified at **Wis. Stat. § 799.01(1)(d)**
- **Source:** [docs.legis.wisconsin.gov/statutes/statutes/799/01](https://docs.legis.wisconsin.gov/statutes/statutes/799/01); [wicourts.gov/services/public/selfhelp/smallclaims.htm](https://www.wicourts.gov/services/public/selfhelp/smallclaims.htm)
- **Important sub-limit:** **Personal-injury and tort actions are capped at $5,000** in small claims (§ 799.01(1)(cr)). A pure billing dispute / contract counterclaim is "other civil," so the $10,000 cap applies. A separate emotional-distress count tied to a debt-collection abuse claim could trip the $5,000 cap — split filings or remove to regular Circuit Court if needed.
- **Filing fees:** Typical $94.50 filing fee plus service costs. Varies slightly by county.
- **Attorney rules:** Attorneys are **permitted** but not required. Wisconsin small claims procedure (chapter 799) is **simplified** — informal pleadings, relaxed evidence rules, accelerated scheduling. Unlike Georgia's Magistrate Court, Wisconsin **does** permit corporations to appear through a full agent (officer or salaried employee) under § 799.06(2) for amounts under the small-claims cap; otherwise an attorney is needed.
- **Jury trial:** Available on demand with appropriate jury fee.

## Statute of limitations

- **Contracts (express or implied), written or oral:** **6 years from breach** — **Wis. Stat. § 893.43(1)**
- **Sources:** [docs.legis.wisconsin.gov/document/statutes/893.43](https://docs.legis.wisconsin.gov/document/statutes/893.43); [law.justia.com/codes/wisconsin/chapter-893/section-893-43](https://law.justia.com/codes/wisconsin/chapter-893/section-893-43/)
- **Notable feature:** Wisconsin applies the **same 6-year clock to oral and written contracts** — unlike Georgia's 6/4 split. This is unusually patient-favorable for hospital bills where no signed financial-responsibility agreement exists.
- **Accrual:** Cause of action accrues at **time of breach**, not discovery. The discovery rule does **not** apply to contract claims (per *Steadfast Ins. Co. v. Greenwich Ins. Co.*).
- **DTPA (§ 100.18):** **3 years** from the unlawful act — § 100.18(11)(b)3. Shorter than contract; file the DTPA count promptly.
- **Acknowledgment / partial payment:** Under § 893.45, a written acknowledgment of a debt or a part-payment can restart the clock as to the acknowledging party. **Do not make a partial payment on a time-barred debt without legal advice.**

## Ground ambulance balance-billing

**No Wisconsin state protection as of this writing.** The federal No Surprises Act expressly **excludes** ground ambulance. Wisconsin Statute § 632.795 is **not** the ambulance provision — it governs open enrollment upon insurer liquidation, despite its number occasionally being miscited in this context.

**2025 Senate Bill 711** ("Coverage and reimbursement of emergency ambulance services") was introduced December 2, 2025 and is pending in the Senate Committee on Insurance, Housing, Rural Issues and Forestry. If enacted, it would set a payment-rate floor for OON ground ambulance and limit patient cost-sharing to in-network amounts. **Verify enactment status before relying on it** at [docs.legis.wisconsin.gov/2025/related/proposals/sb711](https://docs.legis.wisconsin.gov/2025/related/proposals/sb711).

Until enacted, the Wisconsin lever set for an OON ground-ambulance balance bill is:

1. *Anderson* bad faith against the insurer if the insurer is unreasonably refusing the claim,
2. § 100.18 against the ambulance provider for any misleading billing representations,
3. Direct negotiation citing the federal Medicare rate as a benchmark.

## Credit reporting — medical debt

Wisconsin has **not** enacted a state-specific medical-debt credit-reporting restriction. **Wisconsin AB 786 (2023-2024)** would have prohibited reporting of medical-debt collections to consumer reporting agencies but **failed to pass** on April 15, 2024.

Patients in Wisconsin rely on:

- The 2022-2023 voluntary changes by Equifax/Experian/TransUnion (paid medical debt removed; debt under $500 not reported; 1-year delay before reporting).
- Federal FCRA dispute rights (15 U.S.C. §§ 1681i, 1681s-2).
- The Wisconsin Consumer Act (Wis. Stat. ch. 427) prohibition on disclosing or threatening to disclose information adversely affecting credit worthiness "with knowledge or reason to know that the information is false" (§ 427.104(1)(h)). This reaches the **collection agency**, not the original hospital.

**Federal preemption posture is in flux.** The CFPB issued an October 20, 2025 interpretive rule taking the position that the FCRA preempts state laws restricting medical-debt credit reporting, and a federal court has vacated the CFPB's January 2025 medical-debt rule on similar preemption grounds. If those positions survive, they would have limited any Wisconsin state law restricting medical-debt reporting even if AB 786 had passed.

For deceptive furnishing of medical-debt information, § 100.18 may apply to the furnisher's representations to the patient, and an FCRA furnisher claim may lie under 15 U.S.C. § 1681s-2.

## Hospital charity care

Wisconsin has **no state statute** requiring a hospital financial-assistance policy beyond federal law. Non-profit hospitals are bound by **IRS § 501(r)** (federal); for-profit and county/municipal hospitals have no state FAP mandate.

State-side adjacent programs that affect billing leverage:

- **BadgerCare Plus** (Wis. Stat. § 49.471) — the state's expanded Medicaid program, covers adults up to 100% FPL plus children, pregnant women, and the medically needy. Retroactive enrollment up to 3 months can sometimes cover the date of service for an unpaid hospital bill. State page: [dhs.wisconsin.gov/medicaid](https://www.dhs.wisconsin.gov/medicaid/waiver-badgercare1115.htm).
- **Disproportionate Share Hospital (DSH) supplemental payments** — Wisconsin's DSH program supplements hospitals serving high-Medicaid populations. Hospitals receiving DSH funds are practically expected to maintain charity-care policies, even though no state statute mandates it.
- **DHS 124.06 patient-rights provisions** require hospitals to inform patients of available financial-assistance programs on request.

Screening tool: Dollar For at [dollarfor.org/state_sheet/wisconsin](https://dollarfor.org/state_sheet/wisconsin/).

## Hospital lien statute

- **Citation:** **Wis. Stat. § 779.80**
- **Sources:** [docs.legis.wisconsin.gov/document/statutes/779.80](https://docs.legis.wisconsin.gov/document/statutes/779.80); [law.justia.com/codes/wisconsin/chapter-779/section-779-80](https://law.justia.com/codes/wisconsin/chapter-779/section-779-80/)
- **Substance:** A hospital operating as a charitable institution has a lien for reasonable and necessary charges for services rendered to a person who sustained personal injuries due to the negligence or tort of another. The lien attaches **only to the patient's cause of action, judgment, award, or settlement against the third-party tortfeasor**. **Not a lien on the patient's home, wages, or bank accounts.**
- **Perfection requirements:**
  - File a written notice of lien with the **clerk of circuit court** for the county where the hospital is located, before any payment is made to the injured person or representative, and **no later than 60 days after discharge**.
  - The notice must state the patient's name and address, date and location of the injury event, hospital name and location, and the names and addresses (if ascertainable by reasonable diligence) of the alleged tortfeasors.
  - **Within 10 days of filing**, the hospital must send a copy of the notice by certified or registered mail (or by personal service) to the patient and to the alleged tortfeasor, and to the tortfeasor's liability insurer if ascertainable.
- **Defect = void lien:** Failure to send the 10-day notice when the patient's or tortfeasor's name and address are known (or reasonably knowable) **voids the lien** by the statute's own terms. Always demand and inspect the filed notice and proof of certified service before treating the lien as enforceable.
- **Limitation:** Wisconsin hospital liens reach only third-party tort recoveries — they do **not** attach to the patient's first-party UIM/UM proceeds or to workers' compensation benefits as a general rule, though insurer-side coordination-of-benefits issues can complicate this.

## Wage garnishment

- **Statute:** **Wis. Stat. ch. 812**; exemption at **§ 812.34**
- **Substance:** Medical-bill creditors cannot garnish wages without first obtaining a court judgment. Post-judgment, **80% of disposable earnings are exempt** — i.e., garnishment is capped at **20% of disposable earnings** (more protective than the federal CCPA's 25% floor; Wisconsin uses the more-favorable state rule per § 812.34(2)(b)).
- **Complete exemption:** Available if the debtor's household income is below the federal poverty line, or if the debtor receives need-based public assistance (or received it within 6 months, or has been determined eligible) — § 812.34(2)(c). Garnishment is also limited so that disposable income does not fall below the poverty line.
- **Use:** In response letters to collectors threatening garnishment without a judgment in hand, and to document the 80% exemption in any negotiation.

## Wisconsin Consumer Act — debt collection

- **Statute:** **Wis. Stat. ch. 427**; key prohibited-practices section at **§ 427.104**
- **Sources:** [docs.legis.wisconsin.gov/statutes/statutes/427](https://docs.legis.wisconsin.gov/statutes/statutes/427); [law.justia.com/codes/wisconsin/chapter-427/section-427-104](https://law.justia.com/codes/wisconsin/chapter-427/section-427-104/)
- **Substance:** Wisconsin's analog to the federal FDCPA, but **broader** in two respects: it covers the **original creditor** (not just third-party collectors), and it applies to consumer transactions up to $25,000. Prohibits threats of force, contacting the debtor's employer except in narrow circumstances, communicating with third parties about the debt, contacting the debtor at unreasonable times or places, using obscene language, falsely representing the legal status of the debt, and disclosing or threatening to disclose information adversely affecting credit worthiness with knowledge or reason to know that the information is false (§ 427.104(1)(h)).
- **Private right of action:** Yes, under **§ 427.105 / § 425.305**. Remedies: **actual damages** (including emotional-distress and consequential damages), a **statutory penalty of twice the finance charge up to $1,000**, attorney's fees and costs, and possible cancellation of the transaction in egregious cases.
- **Why it matters in Wisconsin:** Reaches the **hospital's in-house billing department** directly, the same gap that Georgia's FBPA closes via § 10-1-393. Cite Wis. Stat. § 427.104 alongside § 100.18 in any 30-day warning letter to a Wisconsin provider's billing office.

- **State insurance department (CC line):** Office of the Commissioner of Insurance, P.O. Box 7873, Madison, WI 53707-7873 ([oci.wi.gov](https://oci.wi.gov))
- **State consumer protection (CC line):** Wisconsin DATCP, Bureau of Consumer Protection, P.O. Box 8911, Madison, WI 53708-8911 ([datcp.wi.gov](https://datcp.wi.gov))

- **Statute of limitations (in 30-day warning):** "Wis. Stat. § 893.43 (six years for breach of contract, written or oral)"

Stat. § 895.043** (capped at greater of $200,000 or 2x compensatory). Reference Wis. Admin. Code § Ins 6.11 as evidentiary support for unreasonableness, not as a standalone count.

## Key Wisconsin-specific advantages

Worth keeping in mind when triaging a WI patient's bills:

1. **Anderson punitives** — Wisconsin is one of the relatively few states recognizing a first-party bad-faith **tort** (not just contract / statutory penalty) with punitives available. Against a fully-insured carrier, this is materially more leverage than Georgia's § 33-4-6 50%-of-loss penalty cap. Always flag in the warning letter to a Wisconsin-licensed insurer.
2. **§ 100.18 mandatory fee shift** — even modest pecuniary loss carries an attorney-fee award on success, which is the realistic enforcement engine for billing disputes too small for contingency-fee work.
3. **Wisconsin Consumer Act original-creditor reach** — § 427.104 reaches the hospital's in-house billing department, not just third-party collectors. Mirrors the Georgia FBPA's reach for the same kind of leverage.
4. **6-year clock on oral contracts** — unusually patient-favorable when no signed financial-responsibility form exists.
5. **80% wage-garnishment exemption** — more protective than the federal CCPA's 25% cap. Note in any letter responding to garnishment threats.
6. **The gap to watch** — no state surprise-billing act, no state ground-ambulance protection (as of this writing), and § 146.83 doesn't cover billing records. Don't promise patients leverage that Wisconsin doesn't provide.
