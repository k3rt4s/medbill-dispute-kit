# Ohio state pack

The fully-worked state-law layer for Ohio patients. The LLM uses this when the patient's state is Ohio. Tennessee equivalent at [`laws_state_tn.md`](laws_state_tn.md); Georgia equivalent at [`laws_state_ga.md`](laws_state_ga.md); New York equivalent at [`laws_state_ny.md`](laws_state_ny.md). All citations verified against public sources as of 2026-05-18. Re-verify annually.

Three things make Ohio's patient-side leverage unusually strong:

1. The **Ohio Consumer Sales Practices Act** (OCSPA), Ohio Rev. Code § 1345.01 et seq., reaches the hospital itself (not just third-party debt collectors). The Cuyahoga County Court of Common Pleas held in *Brakle v. Cleveland Clinic Foundation* (Jan. 14, 2021) that the OCSPA's physician/dentist exclusion does **not** shield hospital and clinic corporate entities — they are "suppliers" subject to treble-damage liability with attorney's fees under § 1345.09.
2. The **Hospital Care Assurance Program** (HCAP), Ohio Rev. Code §§ 5168.01-.14, requires every HCAP-funded hospital to provide basic, medically necessary services **free of charge** to Ohio residents at or below 100% FPL who are not on Medicaid. This is a statutory free-care floor, not a discretionary charity-care policy.
3. **Ohio's surprise-billing law, Ohio Rev. Code § 3902.50 et seq.** (HB 388, effective Jan. 12, 2022), covers **ground ambulance** balance billing for state-regulated fully-insured plans. The federal No Surprises Act does not cover ground ambulance, so this closes the single biggest balance-billing gap in federal law for Ohio patients with fully-insured coverage.

Treat these three as the structural headlines in any Ohio dispute strategy.

## Hospital itemization right

- **Citation:** **Ohio Admin. Code Rule 3701-83-07(A)(5)** ("Patient care policies"), promulgated by the Ohio Department of Health under Ohio Rev. Code Chapter 3727 (hospital registration and licensure). The Ohio Revised Code itself does **not** contain a dedicated statutory itemization section — Ohio's right rides on the administrative rule plus the OCSPA's deceptive-acts framework.
- **Source:** [codes.ohio.gov/ohio-administrative-code/rule-3701-83-07](https://codes.ohio.gov/ohio-administrative-code/rule-3701-83-07); patient-facing examples at [uhhospitals.org/.../request-itemized-bill](https://www.uhhospitals.org/patients-and-visitors/billing-insurance-and-medical-records/pay-your-bill/customer-requests-and-forms/request-itemized-bill) and [somc.org/patients-visitors/request-itemized-bill](https://www.somc.org/patients-visitors/request-itemized-bill/)
- **What it requires:** "Each patient receives, if requested, a detailed explanation of facility charges including an itemized bill for services received." The duty is **triggered by patient request** (unlike Georgia, where the 6-day duty is automatic).
- **Scope:** All hospitals licensed/registered with the Ohio Department of Health under Chapter 3727. Outpatient physician practices are not bound by this rule but are bound by federal price-transparency rules and the federal Good Faith Estimate requirement for uninsured/self-pay patients.
- **Common-citation traps:** Public-facing sources sometimes cite **§ 1751.78** (which actually addresses HMO utilization review — unrelated to itemization) or **§ 3727.42** (which does not exist). Do not use either citation in a dispute letter. Cite Ohio Admin. Code Rule 3701-83-07 and pair it with an OCSPA deceptive-acts theory.
- **Enforcement:** Health-facility licensing complaints go to the Ohio Department of Health; substantive billing-deception complaints go to the Ohio Attorney General's Consumer Protection Section under the OCSPA (see § 1345.09 below).

## OCSPA — applied to hospitals

- **Statutes:** **Ohio Rev. Code § 1345.01** (definitions), **§ 1345.02** (deceptive acts prohibited), **§ 1345.03** (unconscionable acts prohibited), **§ 1345.09** (consumer remedies)
- **Sources:** [codes.ohio.gov/ohio-revised-code/section-1345.01](https://codes.ohio.gov/ohio-revised-code/section-1345.01); [codes.ohio.gov/ohio-revised-code/section-1345.09](https://codes.ohio.gov/ohio-revised-code/section-1345.09); AG plain-English guide at [ohioattorneygeneral.gov/.../Consumer-Sales-Practices-Act-(PDF).aspx](https://www.ohioattorneygeneral.gov/Files/Publications-Files/Publications-for-Business/Consumer-Sales-Practices-Act-(PDF).aspx)
- **Coverage of hospitals:** § 1345.01(A) excludes "transactions between attorneys, physicians, or dentists and their clients or patients" from the definition of "consumer transaction." That exclusion is read narrowly. In ***Brakle v. Cleveland Clinic Foundation*** (Cuyahoga C.P., Jan. 14, 2021), the court held that a hospital corporate entity is "clearly outside of the definition of 'physician'" and is therefore a "supplier" subject to the OCSPA. Source: [consumerfinancialserviceslawmonitor.com/2021/01/ohio-state-court-rules-...](https://www.consumerfinancialserviceslawmonitor.com/2021/01/ohio-state-court-rules-that-healthcare-clinics-and-hospitals-are-not-exempt-from-ohios-consumer-sales-practices-act/); practitioner summary at [bmdllc.com/.../ohio-hospitals-and-healthcare-clinics...](https://www.bmdllc.com/resources/blog/ohio-hospitals-and-healthcare-clinics-it-s-time-to-revisit-your-billing-and-collection-practices/).
- **Reach over original creditors:** This is the headline Ohio-specific advantage. The OCSPA covers **suppliers**, which includes the hospital's in-house billing department itself, not only third-party debt collectors. The federal FDCPA reaches only third-party collectors, so the OCSPA fills the gap.
- **Remedies under § 1345.09:** Actual damages; or **treble damages or $200, whichever is greater**, when the practice was previously declared deceptive by an AG rule or court decision (per § 1345.09(B)); rescission as an alternative; and reasonable attorney's fees against a supplier who knowingly violated the chapter (§ 1345.09(F)(2)).
- **Caveats:**
  - Treble damages under § 1345.09(B) generally require that the specific practice was either declared deceptive by a prior AG rule or court decision; classwide allegations often satisfy this through reference to OAC § 109:4-3 rules.
  - The *Brakle* ruling is trial-court level; the Ohio Supreme Court has not squarely resolved hospital coverage. Pre-suit, lean on it as persuasive authority backed by the broad statutory text.
  - Some hospital defendants have argued that the OCSPA's exclusion of "physicians" should extend to all medical providers. Decline that read in dispute correspondence and cite *Brakle* plus the literal statutory text.

## Insurance unfair-trade-practices framework

- **Statutes:** **Ohio Rev. Code §§ 3901.19-3901.26** — Ohio's Unfair and Deceptive Acts or Practices in the Business of Insurance Act. Key sections:
  - **§ 3901.20**: blanket prohibition on any unfair-or-deceptive act in the business of insurance defined in §§ 3901.19-3901.23.
  - **§ 3901.21**: list of specifically prohibited practices (misrepresenting policy terms, false advertising, etc.).
  - **§ 3901.22**: defines unfair claims-settlement practices (failure to acknowledge claims promptly, failure to adopt reasonable investigation standards, etc.).
- **Sources:** [codes.ohio.gov/ohio-revised-code/section-3901.20](https://codes.ohio.gov/ohio-revised-code/section-3901.20); [codes.ohio.gov/ohio-revised-code/section-3901.21](https://codes.ohio.gov/ohio-revised-code/section-3901.21)
- **Critical caveat — no private right of action:** Ohio courts have held repeatedly that §§ 3901.20-3901.21 do **not** create a private cause of action — they are enforced by the Ohio Superintendent of Insurance. Cite these sections in a complaint to the Ohio Department of Insurance and as evidence supporting a common-law bad-faith claim (see next section), not as a standalone cause of action in court.
- **Use:** Always file the ODI complaint in parallel with any litigation strategy. The ODI complaint is free, generates a paper trail, and frequently moves the carrier in ways litigation cannot.

## Bad-faith failure to pay (common law)

- **Posture:** Ohio recognizes a **common-law tort** of bad-faith handling of an insured's claim — there is no first-party bad-faith statute comparable to Georgia's O.C.G.A. § 33-4-6.
- **Sources:**
  - ***Hoskins v. Aetna Life Ins. Co.***, 6 Ohio St. 3d 272, 452 N.E.2d 1315 (1983) — establishes the tort and authorizes punitive damages on proof of actual malice, fraud, or insult. [leagle.com/decision/19832786ohiost3d2721216](https://www.leagle.com/decision/19832786ohiost3d2721216)
  - ***Zoppo v. Homestead Ins. Co.***, 71 Ohio St. 3d 552, 644 N.E.2d 397 (1994) — refined the standard, removing the "intentional" element and adopting "lack of reasonable justification" as the touchstone for bad faith. Practitioner summary at [ohio-insurance-lawyer.com/insurance-bad-faith-ohio-zoppo-v-homestead](https://www.ohio-insurance-lawyer.com/insurance-bad-faith-ohio-zoppo-v-homestead.php)
- **Elements:** (1) the insurer owed a duty of good faith in handling/paying the insured's claim; (2) the insurer's refusal to pay (or delay) was without reasonable justification under the circumstances known to it at the time; (3) damages flowing from the breach.
- **Damages:** Compensatory damages (policy proceeds plus consequential damages such as financial harm, emotional distress in egregious cases). **Punitive damages** are available under *Hoskins* upon clear-and-convincing proof of actual malice, fraud, or insult (Ohio Rev. Code § 2315.21(D) caps and procedure apply). No statutory penalty. Attorney's fees may be available as an element of compensatory damages where punitives are awarded.
- **ERISA preemption:** Ohio common-law bad-faith claims are **preempted** as applied to self-funded ERISA employer plans. For ERISA self-funded plans, the federal remedy is 29 U.S.C. § 1132(a)(1)(B) plus possible § 1132(g) attorney's fees — no state bad-faith tort. The tort is in play for fully-insured plans, Medicaid managed care, and individual/marketplace plans.

## Surprise billing — Ohio Rev. Code § 3902.50 et seq.

- **Statutes:** **Ohio Rev. Code §§ 3902.50-3902.54** — surprise-billing protections enacted by **HB 388** of the 133rd General Assembly.
- **Effective date:** **January 12, 2022** (signed Jan. 7, 2021; effective one year later).
- **Sources:** [codes.ohio.gov/ohio-revised-code/section-3902.50](https://codes.ohio.gov/ohio-revised-code/section-3902.50); [codes.ohio.gov/ohio-revised-code/section-3902.51](https://codes.ohio.gov/ohio-revised-code/section-3902.51); enacted-bill PDF at [insurance.ohio.gov/static/Consumer/Surprise+Billing/HB388_133_ENACTED.pdf](https://insurance.ohio.gov/static/Consumer/Surprise+Billing/HB388_133_ENACTED.pdf); practitioner summary at [bmdllc.com/resources/blog/federal-and-ohio-laws-on-surprise-billing](https://www.bmdllc.com/resources/blog/federal-and-ohio-laws-on-surprise-billing/)

### What it covers

- **Emergency services** from an out-of-network (OON) provider at any emergency facility (in- or out-of-network).
- **Unanticipated out-of-network care** — non-emergency services rendered by an OON provider at an **in-network** facility (anesthesia, radiology, pathology, lab, neonatology, hospitalist, etc.).
- **Ground ambulance** services from an OON provider in connection with emergency services or unanticipated OON care — Ohio is one of the states that closes the federal NSA's biggest gap.
- **Clinical laboratory services** provided in connection with emergency services or unanticipated OON care.

### Patient hold-harmless and reimbursement rule

- Patient cost-sharing capped at the **in-network** amount; counts toward the in-network deductible and out-of-pocket maximum.
- Default health-plan reimbursement to the OON provider is the **greater** of: (i) the median in-network rate negotiated with participating providers for that service in that geographic region, (ii) the rate the plan would pay for OON services under the benefit plan, or (iii) the **Medicare rate** for the service.
- Provider may decline the default and invoke the negotiation procedure under § 3902.51(C)-(E); the patient is not a party to that dispute.

### Caveats

- **ERISA preemption:** the Act does not reach self-funded ERISA employer plans; those are covered (if at all) only by the federal No Surprises Act. **The federal NSA does not cover ground ambulance**, so Ohio patients on self-funded plans have no protection against OON ground-ambulance balance billing. This is the single most important gating question before invoking § 3902.50 et seq.
- Excludes air ambulance (federal NSA covers it), workers' compensation, Medicare, and Medicaid.
- Only applies to health plans regulated by Ohio (i.e., fully insured).
- A provider can still balance bill if (a) the patient is given written notice of OON status and a good-faith estimate **before** the service and (b) the patient consents in writing — the same notice-and-consent escape hatch as the federal NSA. Bills lacking that paper trail are presumptively unenforceable.
- **HB 388 was the original 2021 enactment, not a 2023 amendment.** Treat any "2023 HB 388 ground-ambulance amendment" reference as a citation error — ground ambulance has been covered since the Act's January 12, 2022 effective date.

## Small claims court — Municipal/County Court Small Claims Division

- **Court name:** **Small Claims Division** of the **Municipal Court** (in cities) or **County Court** (in rural counties). Each county has one or the other based on county designation under Ohio Rev. Code §§ 1901.01, 1907.01.
- **Jurisdictional limit:** **$6,000**, codified at **Ohio Rev. Code § 1925.02(A)(1)**, excluding interest and costs. The limit was raised from $3,000 in 2011. As of the most recent codification, the $6,000 cap was reaffirmed effective April 9, 2025. Source: [codes.ohio.gov/ohio-revised-code/section-1925.02](https://codes.ohio.gov/ohio-revised-code/section-1925.02)
- **Filing fees:** typical 2024-2025 ranges **$50-$95** initial filing including service on one defendant; varies by Municipal/County Court. Check the local clerk's website (e.g., Franklin County Municipal Court, Cuyahoga County Common Pleas referrals, Hamilton County Municipal Court).
- **Attorney rules:** permitted, not required. **Corporate defendants** (including hospitals and provider entities) may appear through a bona fide officer or salaried employee under **Ohio Rev. Code § 1925.17** — **but** that non-attorney representative may not engage in cross-examination, argument, or other acts of advocacy. Practical effect: the patient is more likely to face a billing-department staffer than a defense attorney, and that staffer's hands are tied procedurally. Source: [codes.ohio.gov/ohio-revised-code/section-1925.17](https://codes.ohio.gov/ohio-revised-code/section-1925.17)
- **Jury trial:** not available in small claims division. Either party may transfer the case to the regular docket within the time set by local rule to obtain a jury trial.

## Statute of limitations

- **Written contracts:** **6 years from breach** — **Ohio Rev. Code § 2305.06** (as amended by **SB 13** of the 134th General Assembly, signed Mar. 16, 2021, effective **June 14, 2021**). Source: [codes.ohio.gov/ohio-revised-code/section-2305.06](https://codes.ohio.gov/ohio-revised-code/section-2305.06)
- **Oral contracts:** **4 years from breach** — **Ohio Rev. Code § 2305.07(A)** (also amended by SB 13). Source: [codes.ohio.gov/ohio-revised-code/section-2305.07](https://codes.ohio.gov/ohio-revised-code/section-2305.07)
- **Liability created by statute (other than forfeiture/penalty):** 6 years — § 2305.07(B). This is the relevant clock for OCSPA actions premised on statutory violations.
- **Consumer transactions for personal/family/household purposes:** 6 years from 30 days after the last charge or payment (whichever is later) — § 2305.07(C). This is the relevant clock for ordinary consumer-credit and medical-debt accrued balances.

**SoL amendment history (often miscited):** Ohio's written-contract limitation was 15 years from 1853 until **HB 274 of the 129th GA** (effective Sept. 28, 2012) shortened it to **8 years**, and then SB 13 (effective June 14, 2021) shortened it again to **6 years**. The "8-year" figure is no longer current — use 6 years in any dispute letter for breach accruing on or after June 14, 2021. For pre-June-14-2021 accruals, the bridge rule applies: the claim must be brought within the time remaining under the prior 8-year period **or by June 14, 2027, whichever is earlier**. Source: [hahnlaw.com/insights/ohio-bill-shortening-statute-of-limitations-goes-into-effect](https://www.hahnlaw.com/insights/ohio-bill-shortening-statute-of-limitations-goes-into-effect/); [bmdllc.com/resources/blog/ohio-breach-of-contract-statute-of-limitations-shortened-to-6-years](https://www.bmdllc.com/resources/blog/ohio-breach-of-contract-statute-of-limitations-shortened-to-6-years/)

Most hospital admissions involve a signed financial-responsibility form — a written contract, so 6 years applies. Implied-in-fact arrangements without a signed agreement may be treated as oral contracts (4 years). The clock runs from breach (typically the day payment was due and not made), not from when damages are discovered.

Partial payment or written acknowledgment of the debt may restart the clock under common-law principles. **Do not make a partial payment on a time-barred debt without legal advice.**

## Ground-ambulance protection

**Covered by Ohio state law since January 12, 2022**, via Ohio Rev. Code §§ 3902.50-3902.54 as enacted by HB 388. See "Surprise billing" above.

For policies issued, delivered, or renewed in Ohio that are regulated by the Ohio Department of Insurance, an insured patient receiving ground ambulance from an out-of-network provider in connection with emergency services or unanticipated OON care may be billed no more than the in-network cost-share. This is the single most important Ohio-specific protection in this category because the **federal No Surprises Act explicitly excludes ground ambulance**.

ERISA-preempted for self-funded employer plans; excludes air ambulance, workers' comp, Medicare, Medicaid. Before invoking the statute, confirm the patient's plan is fully insured and Ohio-regulated, not a self-funded ERISA plan (the plan summary/ID card or HR can confirm; if uncertain, ask in writing).

**Common-citation trap:** Public-facing sources sometimes describe HB 388 as a "2023 amendment adding ground ambulance." That is incorrect. Ground ambulance was in the original 2021 bill and has been live since the January 12, 2022 effective date. Do not cite a 2023 ground-ambulance amendment that does not exist.

## Credit reporting

Ohio has not enacted a state-specific medical-debt credit-reporting restriction as of this writing. **HB 257** (136th General Assembly, the "Ohio Medical Debt Fairness Act") would, among other things, cap medical-debt interest at 3% per annum, prohibit reporting medical debt to consumer reporting agencies, and ban wage garnishment for medical debt. The bill was bipartisan (Reps. Grim and Schmidt) but had not been enacted as of this writing — check enactment status before relying on it. Source: [legislature.ohio.gov/legislation/136/hb257](https://www.legislature.ohio.gov/legislation/136/hb257); [elkandelk.com/.../ohio-medical-debt-fairness-act](https://www.elkandelk.com/understanding-the-ohio-medical-debt-fairness-act-what-it-means-for-you/)

Patients in Ohio currently rely on:

- The 2022-2023 voluntary changes by Equifax/Experian/TransUnion (paid medical debt removed; debt under $500 not reported; 1-year delay before reporting).
- Federal FCRA dispute rights (15 U.S.C. §§ 1681i, 1681s-2).
- The OCSPA's deceptive-acts framework as applied to furnishing inaccurate medical-debt information to credit bureaus, via § 1345.02 (deceptive acts) and § 1345.09 remedies. This is an Ohio-specific lever not available in most states.

**Federal preemption posture is in flux.** The CFPB issued an October 2025 interpretive rule taking the position that the FCRA preempts state laws restricting medical-debt credit reporting. If that survives challenge, it limits the effect of any state-level law Ohio might pass.

## Hospital charity care — Hospital Care Assurance Program (HCAP)

- **Statutes:** **Ohio Rev. Code §§ 5168.01-5168.14** — Hospital Care Assurance Program. Key sections: § 5168.07 (intergovernmental transfers / funding mechanism for governmental hospitals); **§ 5168.14 (the free-care duty itself)**.
- **Sources:** [codes.ohio.gov/ohio-revised-code/chapter-5168](https://codes.ohio.gov/ohio-revised-code/chapter-5168); [codes.ohio.gov/ohio-revised-code/section-5168.14](https://codes.ohio.gov/ohio-revised-code/section-5168.14); program description at [ohiohospitals.org/Health-Economics/Hospital-Finance/HCAP](https://www.ohiohospitals.org/Health-Economics/Hospital-Finance/HCAP); patient-facing example at [uhhospitals.org/.../hcap-hospital-care-assurance-program-guidelines](https://www.uhhospitals.org/patients-and-visitors/billing-insurance-and-medical-records/pay-your-bill/financial-assistance/hcap-hospital-care-assurance-program-guidelines)
- **Substance:** Each hospital that receives HCAP funding **must provide, without charge, basic medically necessary hospital-level services** to any individual who is (i) a resident of Ohio, (ii) not a Medicaid recipient, and (iii) has income at or below **100% of the federal poverty guidelines**. The hospital may bill third-party payers and may verify income eligibility before zeroing out a patient balance, but the duty itself is statutory, not discretionary.
- **Headline value:** HCAP is a **statutory free-care floor**, not a discretionary charity-care policy. Almost every Ohio hospital participates because HCAP funds are tied to Medicaid disproportionate-share (DSH) payments. When a patient is at or below 100% FPL, the dispute letter should demand HCAP write-off by name and cite § 5168.14.
- **Caveats:**
  - HCAP covers basic, medically necessary hospital-level services. Outpatient physician charges billed separately by an independent practice group are not covered by HCAP (route those to the physician group's own FAP or to the federal § 501(r) policy if the group is non-profit).
  - Eligibility is a snapshot at the time of service (some hospitals extend look-back periods).
  - Non-participating hospitals (rare) have no HCAP duty.
- **In addition to HCAP**, non-profit hospitals are bound by **IRS § 501(r)** (federal) to have a written financial-assistance policy. Use Dollar For at [dollarfor.org/state_sheet/ohio](https://dollarfor.org/state_sheet/ohio/) for screening.

## Hospital lien — there isn't one

**Ohio is one of a handful of states with no general hospital lien statute.** Hospitals cannot file a statutory lien against a personal-injury settlement, the patient's home, wages, or bank accounts solely by virtue of being a hospital. Source: [216lawyers.com/understanding-medical-liens-in-ohio-personal-injury-cases](https://216lawyers.com/understanding-medical-liens-in-ohio-personal-injury-cases/); [mwl-law.com/.../HOSPITAL-LIEN-LAWS-IN-ALL-50-STATES-CHART](https://www.mwl-law.com/wp-content/uploads/2019/09/HOSPITAL-LIEN-LAWS-IN-ALL-50-STATES-CHART-00215685x9EBBF.pdf)

What does exist in Ohio:

- **Medicaid right of recovery** under **Ohio Rev. Code § 5160.37** — the Ohio Department of Medicaid has a statutory subrogation/right-of-recovery against tort settlements for amounts paid on the patient's behalf. Source: [codes.ohio.gov/ohio-revised-code/section-5160.37](https://codes.ohio.gov/ohio-revised-code/section-5160.37)
- **Workers' Compensation subrogation** under **Ohio Rev. Code §§ 4123.93-.931**.
- **Contract-based "letters of protection"** and subrogation clauses in private health-insurance policies — these are contractual, not statutory liens.

**Note on the prompt-suggested § 2305.27:** Ohio Rev. Code § 2305.27 does not establish a hospital lien (and does not exist as a current section). Do not cite § 2305.27 as a hospital-lien provision in any dispute letter.

**Headline value:** A hospital that asserts a "lien" on a patient's home, wages, or bank account in Ohio is misrepresenting the law. That misrepresentation is itself an OCSPA-actionable deceptive act under § 1345.02. This is a strong Ohio-specific defensive lever.

## Wage garnishment

- **Statutes:** **Ohio Rev. Code § 2716.01 et seq.** (post-judgment wage garnishment generally); **§ 2329.66** (statutory exemptions, including the $500 wage-protection floor and federal minimum-wage protections).
- **Substance:** Medical-bill creditors cannot garnish wages without first obtaining a **court judgment**. Post-judgment, garnishment is capped at the federal Consumer Credit Protection Act floor (25% of disposable earnings, or the amount by which weekly disposable earnings exceed 30× the federal minimum wage, whichever is less). Ohio law layers additional notice and demand requirements before garnishment can issue.
- **Use:** In response letters to collectors threatening garnishment without a judgment in hand, this combined with the OCSPA § 1345.02 deceptive-acts theory is a particularly strong Ohio response — a pre-judgment garnishment threat is both procedurally unenforceable and an actionable OCSPA violation.

## Other Ohio-specific patient advantages

1. **OCSPA reach over original creditors and hospitals (post-*Brakle*).** The single biggest leverage point in Ohio. Most state UDAP statutes either exempt hospitals/medical providers entirely or only reach third-party collectors. Ohio's OCSPA reaches the hospital's in-house billing department, with treble damages and attorney's fees available under § 1345.09.
2. **No hospital lien statute.** Patients in Ohio do not face hospital-asserted liens on their cause-of-action or personal property purely by virtue of treatment. Threats to file a "hospital lien" are deceptive and actionable.
3. **Small-claims advocacy bar on corporate non-attorney representatives** (§ 1925.17). Hospital billing-department staff appearing in small claims can present claims but cannot cross-examine, argue, or otherwise advocate. The patient appearing pro se faces no real cross-examination — the economic and procedural leverage in Ohio small claims is unusually patient-favorable.
4. **HCAP free-care floor at 100% FPL.** Statutory, not discretionary. Cite § 5168.14 by name in any dispute letter for an at-FPL patient.
5. **Ohio ground-ambulance coverage since January 12, 2022.** One of the states that closed the federal NSA's biggest gap — at the date Ohio went live, only a minority of states did so. Always check whether a ground-ambulance bill post-dates Jan. 12, 2022 and whether the patient is on a fully-insured plan.

## Key Ohio-specific advantages

Worth keeping in mind when triaging an Ohio patient's bills:

1. **OCSPA reach over hospitals and original creditors** — the single highest-leverage Ohio cite. Treble damages and attorney's fees against the hospital billing department itself, post-*Brakle*. Mention in any 30-day warning letter to an Ohio provider.
2. **No hospital lien statute** — defensive headline. Any "hospital lien" threat against a settlement, home, wages, or bank account is misrepresented Ohio law and itself an OCSPA-actionable deceptive act.
3. **HCAP statutory free-care duty at 100% FPL** (§ 5168.14) — not a discretionary charity policy, a statutory obligation tied to Medicaid DSH funding. Almost every Ohio hospital participates.
4. **Ground-ambulance coverage since Jan. 12, 2022** (§§ 3902.50-3902.54) — Ohio is one of the states that closed the federal NSA's biggest gap. Always check the service date and plan type.
5. **Small-claims procedural advantage** — corporate defendants can appear through a non-attorney officer or employee under § 1925.17, but that representative may not cross-examine, argue, or advocate. The patient appearing pro se faces no meaningful advocacy from the other side.
