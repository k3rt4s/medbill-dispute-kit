# Oklahoma state pack

The fully-worked state-law layer for Oklahoma patients. The LLM uses this when the patient's state is Oklahoma. Georgia equivalent at [`laws_state_ga.md`](laws_state_ga.md), Tennessee at [`laws_state_tn.md`](laws_state_tn.md). All citations verified against public sources as of 2026-05-19. Re-verify annually.

Three things make Oklahoma's patient-side leverage unusually strong:

1. **Christian bad-faith doctrine** (Christian v. American Home Assur. Co., 1977 OK 141, 577 P.2d 899) is the seminal first-party insurance bad-faith decision in U.S. law. Unreasonable, bad-faith refusal to pay a covered claim is a stand-alone tort, with consequential damages, emotional distress, and punitive damages all available. The doctrine is mature, robust, and patient-friendly.
2. **HB 4148 (2024), codified at 12 O.S. § 193**, conditions any civil action to collect medical debt on the provider's compliance with state and federal hospital price-transparency laws. A non-compliant hospital cannot get a judgment. This is a uniquely strong procedural lever, in effect since November 1, 2024.
3. **The Out-of-Network Ambulance Service Provider Act** closes the federal No Surprises Act's biggest gap (ground ambulance), prohibiting balance billing beyond the in-network cost-share for fully-insured plans.

## Hospital itemization right

- **Statute:** Oklahoma has **no single dedicated hospital-itemization statute** like Georgia's O.C.G.A. § 10-1-393(b)(14) or Tennessee's Tenn. Code Ann. § 68-11-220. The functional equivalents are:
  - **63 O.S. § 1-725.1 et seq.** — **Transparency in Health Care Prices Act** (enacted Laws 2021, c. 371; substantially amended by HB 4148, 2024). Hospitals and providers must publicly post prices for the twenty most common services/DRGs/CPT codes, refreshed at least annually, with plain-English descriptions.
  - **12 O.S. § 193** — Required information for civil action to collect medical debt. Effective November 1, 2024. Any creditor, debt collector, or collection agency filing a civil action for medical debt must **attach a redacted itemization of the charges** that are the basis for the debt **and proof of compliance** with state and federal hospital price-transparency laws.
  - **76 O.S. § 19** — patients are entitled, upon request and tender of expenses ($0.50/page), to copies of all medical bills pertaining to their case. This is the general access right and is the cleanest cite for a written itemization-request letter.
- **Sources:** [law.justia.com/codes/oklahoma/title-63/section-63-1-725-1](https://law.justia.com/codes/oklahoma/title-63/section-63-1-725-1/); [law.justia.com/codes/oklahoma/title-12/section-12-193](https://law.justia.com/codes/oklahoma/title-12/section-12-193/); [law.justia.com/codes/oklahoma/title-76/section-76-19](https://law.justia.com/codes/oklahoma/title-76/section-76-19/); House summary at [okhouse.gov/posts/news-20240521_1](https://www.okhouse.gov/posts/news-20240521_1)
- **What this means in practice:**
  - There is **no automatic 6-business-day or 10-day post-discharge duty** under Oklahoma law. The itemization right operates on a written-request model under § 76-19 plus the public-posting duty under § 1-725.1.
  - Send a written itemization request citing **76 O.S. § 19** for the bills-on-file right and **63 O.S. § 1-725.1 et seq.** for the requirement that posted prices match the bill. Discrepancies between billed charges and posted prices are leverage.
  - **The strongest cite is § 12-193.** If the hospital ever tries to sue, it must produce both the itemization and price-transparency proof, the dispute letter can foreshadow this requirement and put the hospital on notice that suit would expose any compliance gaps.
- **Caveat:** A patient cannot directly enforce § 1-725.1 in a private suit, the consequence flows through § 12-193 (defense to collection) and Oklahoma Consumer Protection Act claims (see below) for deceptive pricing.

**Heads up on the user-supplied cite:** **63 O.S. § 1-715 was repealed by Laws 1999, c. 93, § 10**, effective November 1, 1999. Do not cite it. The current Oklahoma hospital-pricing framework lives at 63 O.S. § 1-725.1 et seq. and 12 O.S. § 193.

## Unfair Claims Settlement Practices Act

- **Statute:** **36 O.S. § 1250.1 et seq.** — Unfair Claims Settlement Practices Act; listed practices at **§ 1250.5**; threshold/application provisions at **§ 1250.3**
- **Source:** [law.justia.com/codes/oklahoma/title-36/section-36-1250-5](https://law.justia.com/codes/oklahoma/title-36/section-36-1250-5/); [law.justia.com/codes/oklahoma/title-36/section-36-1250-3](https://law.justia.com/codes/oklahoma/title-36/section-36-1250-3/); academic treatment at [digitalcommons.law.utulsa.edu/tlr/vol24/iss3/2](https://digitalcommons.law.utulsa.edu/tlr/vol24/iss3/2/)
- **Substance:** Prohibits insurers from engaging in a defined list of unfair claims-settlement practices, knowingly misrepresenting policy provisions, failing to acknowledge claim communications promptly, failing to adopt reasonable standards for prompt investigations, failing in good faith to effectuate prompt fair settlements where liability is reasonably clear, compelling insureds to litigate to recover amounts due, and similar conduct. **§ 1250.3** generally requires the practice to be committed with such frequency as to indicate a general business practice before regulatory enforcement attaches.
- **Critical caveat:** **No private right of action.** The Oklahoma Supreme Court held in *McWhirter v. Fire Ins. Exchange*, 1994 OK 93, 878 P.2d 1056, that violation of the UCSPA does not by itself give rise to a private cause of action. Enforcement is by the Oklahoma Insurance Commissioner.
- **Use:** Cite UCSPA violations in your complaint to the Oklahoma Insurance Department (see below) and as evidentiary support for a Christian bad-faith claim. The Oklahoma Court of Civil Appeals confirmed in *Beers v. Hillory*, 2010 OK CIV APP 99, 241 P.3d 285, that UCSPA violations are relevant to determining the reasonableness of an insurer's conduct in a bad-faith action. **Do not plead UCSPA as a standalone count in litigation.**

## Bad-faith failure to pay (Christian doctrine)

- **Authority:** ***Christian v. American Home Assurance Co.*, 1977 OK 141, 577 P.2d 899** (Okla. Sup. Ct. 1977), the seminal Oklahoma first-party bad-faith case. Codified-by-implication through subsequent Oklahoma Supreme Court decisions (*Buzzard v. Farmers Ins. Co.*, 1991 OK 127; *Badillo v. Mid Century Ins. Co.*, 2005 OK 48).
- **Source:** [law.justia.com/cases/oklahoma/supreme-court/1977/47729.html](https://law.justia.com/cases/oklahoma/supreme-court/1977/47729.html)
- **Substance:** Every insurance contract in Oklahoma carries an implied duty to deal fairly and in good faith with the insured. Unreasonable refusal or unreasonable delay in paying a valid claim is a **stand-alone tort**, not merely breach of contract. The duty exists by operation of law and cannot be contracted around.
- **Standard:** Tort liability attaches where the insurer's conduct was **unreasonable under the circumstances and not based on a legitimate dispute** (the "no reasonable basis" or "objectively unreasonable" test). Mere negligence or honest mistake is not enough. The insurer is entitled to dispute claims that are fairly debatable in good faith.
- **Damages available:**
  - The amount of the underlying covered loss.
  - **Consequential damages** (e.g., financial losses caused by the wrongful denial).
  - **Emotional distress damages.**
  - **Punitive damages** under 23 O.S. § 9.1, with tiered caps: Category I (reckless disregard) at the greater of $100,000 or actual damages; Category II (intentional/malicious) at the greater of $500,000 or actual damages or 2× actual; Category III (intent to injure plus risk to life) uncapped.
  - **Pre-judgment interest** under 36 O.S. § 3629(B) where the insurer fails to pay within 90 days of proof of loss and the insured is the prevailing party.
- **No 60-day pre-suit demand requirement.** Unlike Georgia (O.C.G.A. § 33-4-6) and Tennessee (Tenn. Code Ann. § 56-7-105), Oklahoma's bad-faith doctrine is common-law and does not require a formal pre-suit demand. A written demand is still strategically wise to establish the timeline and the insurer's awareness, but it is not a jurisdictional prerequisite.
- **Coverage:** All insurance policies governed by Oklahoma law, including health insurance, disability, life, and property/casualty. First-party only, *Badillo* (2005) declined to extend Christian to third-party claims.
- **ERISA preemption:** Christian is **preempted** as applied to self-funded ERISA employer plans. The federal remedy is 29 U.S.C. § 1132(a)(1)(B) plus possible § 1132(g) attorney's fees, no Oklahoma tort recovery. Christian is in play for fully-insured employer plans, individual/marketplace plans, Medicare supplements, and most Medicaid managed care (subject to specific federal preemption analyses).

## Surprise billing / Out-of-Network Ambulance Service Provider Act

- **Statute:** **36 O.S. § 6593 et seq.** — Out-of-Network Ambulance Service Provider Act (originally enacted Laws 2021; substantially amended by **HB 2872, signed May 29, 2024**, effective November 1, 2024)
- **Implementing agency:** Oklahoma Insurance Department; rate database due by January 1, 2026
- **Sources:** Oklahoma Insurance Department No Surprises Act portal at [oid.ok.gov/nsa](https://www.oid.ok.gov/nsa/); Commonwealth Fund state survey at [commonwealthfund.org/blog/2024/states-forge-ahead-protect-consumers-advisory-committee-recommends-federal-action](https://www.commonwealthfund.org/blog/2024/states-forge-ahead-protect-consumers-advisory-committee-recommends-federal-action); bill tracking at [billtrack50.com/billdetail/1535605](https://www.billtrack50.com/billdetail/1535605/17934)

### What it prohibits

- **Balance billing for out-of-network ground ambulance services.** Patient cost-sharing is limited to the in-network copay/coinsurance/deductible, and the patient is held harmless from the rate dispute between the insurer and the ambulance provider.
- The ambulance provider may bill the patient only standard in-network copayments and deductibles; the insurer's payment plus those amounts constitutes full payment.

### How the rate is set

- Local governmental entities or ambulance service providers may annually submit rates to the Oklahoma Insurance Department.
- For services where no local rate has been submitted, the minimum reimbursement is **the lesser of 325% of the Medicare rate or the provider's billed charges**.
- OID is building a public database of submitted rates, due online by January 1, 2026; the program structure expires by its own terms on December 31, 2027, unless renewed.

### Beyond ground ambulance

- Oklahoma did **not** enact a comprehensive pre-NSA state surprise-billing statute analogous to Georgia's O.C.G.A. § 33-20E-1 et seq. or California's AB 72. **For non-ambulance surprise bills (out-of-network emergency care at any facility, out-of-network ancillary providers at in-network facilities), the federal No Surprises Act is the operative protection.** See [`laws_federal.md`](laws_federal.md).

### Caveats

- **ERISA preemption:** The Act does not reach self-funded ERISA employer plans. Those are covered (if at all) only by the federal NSA, which does not include ground ambulance.
- Excludes air ambulance (federal NSA covers it), workers' compensation, Medicare, Medicaid.
- Applies only to plans regulated by Oklahoma (fully insured).
- Pre-November 1, 2024 dates of service use the earlier (2021) version of the Act, which had a different rate structure.

## Oklahoma Consumer Protection Act (OCPA)

- **Statute:** **15 O.S. § 751 et seq.** — Oklahoma Consumer Protection Act. Private right of action at **§ 761.1**.
- **Source:** [law.justia.com/codes/oklahoma/title-15/section-15-761-1](https://law.justia.com/codes/oklahoma/title-15/section-15-761-1/); [oscn.net/applications/oscn/DeliverDocument.asp?CiteID=66266](https://www.oscn.net/applications/oscn/DeliverDocument.asp?CiteID=66266)
- **Substance:** Prohibits unfair and deceptive trade practices in consumer transactions, including misrepresenting the price, characteristics, or terms of goods or services. Reaches **original creditors**, including hospitals and their in-house billing departments, not just third-party debt collectors. This is broader than the federal FDCPA.
- **Private right of action under § 761.1:**
  - **Actual damages** sustained by the consumer.
  - **Reasonable attorney's fees and costs of litigation.**
  - **Civil penalty of up to $10,000 per violation** in a private action (in addition to or in lieu of other relief, at the court's discretion).
  - A non-prevailing party who asserted a claim or defense in bad faith may be ordered to reimburse the prevailing party up to $10,000 for costs and fees (§ 761.1(C)).
- **Use:** A hospital that bills for services never rendered, upcodes, misrepresents in-network status, misrepresents charity-care eligibility, or violates posted-price transparency under 63 O.S. § 1-725.1 is exposed to an OCPA claim with **fee recovery**. This makes the threat economically credible in cases where actual damages alone would not justify suit.
- **Caveat:** The statute exempts conduct already regulated by another agency where the regulator has actually exercised jurisdiction (**§ 754**). Pure insurance-claim handling falls under the OID's UCSPA jurisdiction, so OCPA claims work best against the **provider/hospital side** of the dispute, not the insurer side. For insurers, use Christian bad-faith.

## Small claims court

- **Court name:** **Small Claims Division of the District Court** (each county; not a separate court)
- **Statute:** **12 O.S. § 1751** (Small Claims Procedure Act); claim/counterclaim cap at **§ 1759**
- **Jurisdictional limit:** **$10,000**, exclusive of attorney fees and court costs (last amended by Laws 2024, c. 108, § 1, effective November 1, 2024)
- **Source:** [law.justia.com/codes/oklahoma/title-12/section-12-1751](https://law.justia.com/codes/oklahoma/title-12/section-12-1751/); [law.justia.com/codes/oklahoma/title-12/section-12-1759](https://law.justia.com/codes/oklahoma/title-12/section-12-1759/)
- **Scope:** actions for recovery of money based on contract or tort (excluding libel and slander), replevin of personal property up to $10,000, and interpleader up to $10,000.
- **Filing fees:** vary by county. Typical 2024-2025 ranges: claims up to $1,500 around $63-$80; claims $1,500-$10,000 around $85-$105 (Oklahoma County District Court small-claims schedule is representative). Add service-of-process fees of $10-$15 per defendant.
- **Attorney rules:** **permitted, not required.** If attorneys appear and the plaintiff prevails, **attorney fees are capped at 10% of the judgment** unless the court, on a documented application, awards up to 25% (§ 1751.1).
- **Jury trial:** not available in small claims, either party may demand transfer to the regular civil docket if the case warrants jury treatment.
- **Corporate appearance:** corporations and LLCs in Oklahoma small claims may be represented by a non-attorney officer or designated employee, which mirrors the patient-friendly Magistrate Court rule in Georgia.

## Statute of limitations

- **Written contracts:** **5 years from breach** — **12 O.S. § 95(A)(1)**
- **Oral contracts / implied-in-fact:** **3 years from breach** — **12 O.S. § 95(A)(2)**
- **Open accounts / accounts stated:** generally treated under the oral/implied bucket (3 years) unless reduced to a signed written agreement
- **Source:** [law.justia.com/codes/oklahoma/title-12/section-12-95](https://law.justia.com/codes/oklahoma/title-12/section-12-95/)

Most hospital admissions involve a signed financial-responsibility form, **a written contract, so 5 years applies**. Where there is no signed agreement (e.g., emergency-only care where the patient was unable to sign), the bill may fall under the 3-year oral/implied bucket. The clock runs from breach (typically the day payment was due and not made), not from when damages are discovered.

Partial payment or written acknowledgment of the debt can restart the limitation period under standard Oklahoma contract doctrine. **Do not make a partial payment on a time-barred debt without legal advice.**

## Ground ambulance balance-billing

**Covered by Oklahoma state law** under the Out-of-Network Ambulance Service Provider Act, 36 O.S. § 6593 et seq., as amended by HB 2872 (2024). See "Surprise billing / Out-of-Network Ambulance Service Provider Act" above.

For policies issued, delivered, or renewed in Oklahoma on or after November 1, 2024, an insured patient receiving ground ambulance from an out-of-network provider may be billed no more than the in-network cost-share. The patient is held harmless from any rate dispute between insurer and provider, with the insurer paying the local-rate or default 325%-of-Medicare benchmark.

This is the single most important Oklahoma-specific protection beyond the Christian doctrine, because the **federal No Surprises Act explicitly excludes ground ambulance**.

ERISA-preempted for self-funded employer plans; excludes air ambulance, workers' comp, Medicare, Medicaid.

## Medical-debt credit reporting

Oklahoma has **no enacted state statute** restricting medical-debt furnishing to consumer reporting agencies. Two bills (HB 1709 and SB 519) were introduced in the 2025 session to bar reporting of medical debt for lifesaving and emergency care, but **neither advanced from its chamber of origin** as of this writing. Both sponsors stated intent to refile.

Oklahoma patients currently rely on:

- The 2022-2023 voluntary changes by Equifax/Experian/TransUnion (paid medical debt removed; debt under $500 not reported; 1-year delay before reporting).
- Federal FCRA dispute rights (15 U.S.C. §§ 1681i, 1681s-2) against furnishers and bureaus.
- **The CFPB October 2025 interpretive rule** taking the position that the FCRA preempts state laws restricting medical-debt credit reporting. If that position holds, future Oklahoma legislation in this space would have limited force.

For deceptive furnishing of medical-debt information (e.g., reporting a balance that the patient already disputed and the hospital cannot substantiate), the **Oklahoma Consumer Protection Act** § 753 may apply via its deceptive-practices catch-all, paired with a federal FCRA furnisher-liability claim against the hospital under 15 U.S.C. § 1681s-2(b).

## Hospital lien statute

- **Citations:** **42 O.S. §§ 43, 44, 46** (hospital and physician liens, personal-injury context)
- **Sources:** [law.justia.com/codes/oklahoma/title-42/section-42-43](https://law.justia.com/codes/oklahoma/title-42/section-42-43/); [law.justia.com/codes/oklahoma/title-42/section-42-44](https://law.justia.com/codes/oklahoma/title-42/section-42-44/); practitioner overview at [dsda.com/healthcare-successful-hospital-and-physician-liens-depend-on-proper-procedure](https://www.dsda.com/healthcare-successful-hospital-and-physician-liens-depend-on-proper-procedure/)
- **Substance:** Every hospital in Oklahoma furnishing emergency medical or other service to an injured patient has a lien for reasonable and necessary charges, **only against the patient's cause of action / recovery against a third party** who caused the injury (typical case: the at-fault driver in a motor-vehicle accident). **Not a lien on the patient's home, wages, bank accounts, or unrelated assets.** Physician liens under § 46 attach on parallel terms.
- **Perfection requirements (§ 44):**
  - **Notice by registered or certified mail** to the patient at the patient's last known address (if known) and to the patient's attorney (if known to the hospital).
  - Notice to the person, firm, or insurer alleged to be liable to the patient.
  - **Filing of a verified statement** of the lien in the office of the county clerk of the county where the hospital is located, containing the patient's name and address, the dates of treatment, the amount claimed, and the name of the alleged tortfeasor.
- **Enforcement:** by civil action in district court of the county where the lien is filed, within **one year** after the hospital becomes aware of final judgment, settlement, or compromise.
- **Common defenses:** improper notice, untimely or improperly verified filing, unreasonable charges, and (post-2024) failure to comply with § 1-725.1 price transparency as a predicate to enforceability when the issue is litigated under § 12-193.

## Hospital charity care

Oklahoma has **no general statute** mandating a hospital financial-assistance policy for private or for-profit hospitals beyond what federal law already requires. Non-profit hospitals are bound by IRS § 501(r) (federal floor); county and municipal hospitals receiving state indigent-care funds (Title 56 and Title 63 ch. 32 frameworks) have additional obligations under those funding statutes.

Specific Oklahoma frameworks worth flagging in disputes when applicable:

- **Oklahoma Indigent Health Care Act** (Title 56, § 58 et seq.) and the **Community Hospitals Authority** (63 O.S. § 3240.3) — hospitals receiving state indigent-care appropriations have indigent-care service obligations.
- **University Hospitals Authority** (63 O.S. § 3203) — OU Health system facilities must deliver indigent care at or above 120% of appropriations on a Medicare-cost basis.
- **Hospital charge limits for charity-eligible patients:** for hospitals participating in certain state programs, post-discount charges may not exceed the amount Medicare would pay for the same services.

Use Dollar For at [dollarfor.org/state_sheet/oklahoma](https://dollarfor.org/state_sheet/oklahoma/) for charity-care screening across Oklahoma hospitals.

## Wage garnishment

- **Statute:** **12 O.S. §§ 1171, 1173.4** (general garnishment and continuing earnings garnishment); **31 O.S. § 1.1** (homestead and personal-property exemptions)
- **Source:** [law.justia.com/codes/oklahoma/title-12/section-12-1173-4](https://law.justia.com/codes/oklahoma/title-12/section-12-1173-4/)
- **Substance:** Medical-bill creditors cannot garnish wages without first obtaining a court judgment. Post-judgment, **wage garnishment is capped at 25% of disposable earnings** (or the amount by which weekly disposable earnings exceed 30× the federal minimum wage, whichever is less), tracking the federal Consumer Credit Protection Act floor.
- **Oklahoma "75% rule":** under 12 O.S. § 1171.2, 75% of wages earned by the debtor in the 90 days preceding the garnishment are exempt (except for child-support garnishments). This is an Oklahoma-specific exemption layered on top of the standard CCPA cap and benefits low-wage debtors most.
- **Continuing earnings garnishments** under § 1173.4 last until satisfaction or **180 days, whichever comes first**, then must be renewed.
- **Use:** in response letters to collectors threatening garnishment without a judgment in hand, and in hardship narratives where the debtor's wages would fall within the 75% protection.

## Key Oklahoma-specific advantages

Worth keeping in mind when triaging an OK patient's bills:

1. **Christian bad-faith doctrine** is uniquely robust. Punitive damages, emotional-distress damages, and fee shifting under fee-shifting statutes apply in successful bad-faith actions, with no 60-day pre-suit demand requirement. Insurers know the doctrine well, and a credible written demand referencing Christian carries weight.
2. **12 O.S. § 193 / HB 4148** is a uniquely strong procedural lever. A hospital out of compliance with state or federal price-transparency rules **cannot get a judgment**, which means it cannot effectively collect a disputed bill in court. Mention in any 30-day warning letter to OK providers, especially ones whose chargemaster postings are stale or incomplete.
3. **Ground ambulance** is covered by state law (HB 2872, effective Nov 1, 2024). Always check whether the ambulance date of service post-dates the effective date.
4. **OCPA fee recovery + $10,000 civil penalty** under 15 O.S. § 761.1 makes provider-side disputes economically credible even when actual damages are modest. The OCPA reach over original creditors (the hospital itself) is broader than the federal FDCPA.
5. **Oklahoma "75% rule" wage exemption** (12 O.S. § 1171.2) means low-wage debtors keep more than the federal CCPA floor would allow. Useful in hardship-narrative sections of dispute letters and in negotiations over post-judgment collection.
