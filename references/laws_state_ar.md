# Arkansas state pack

The fully-worked state-law layer for Arkansas patients. The LLM uses this when the patient's state is Arkansas. Georgia equivalent at [`laws_state_ga.md`](laws_state_ga.md), Alabama at [`laws_state_al.md`](laws_state_al.md), Oklahoma at [`laws_state_ok.md`](laws_state_ok.md). All citations verified against public sources as of 2026-05-19. Re-verify annually.

Three things make Arkansas's patient-side leverage unusually strong, and one ordinary lever is uncommonly weak:

1. **Medical-debt-specific 2-year statute of limitations** at **Ark. Code § 16-56-106** is shorter than the general 5-year written-contract limit at § 16-56-111. Arkansas is one of a small number of states with a special, short medical-debt SOL. Always pin the SOL on § 16-56-106 first when defending a collection action.
2. **No attorneys allowed in small claims** under **Ark. Code § 16-17-606**. Arkansas is one of the strictest no-lawyer small-claims jurisdictions in the country. A corporate defendant must appear through a non-attorney officer or employee, which compresses the hospital's defense economics and shifts leverage toward the self-represented patient.
3. **Ground-ambulance balance-billing protection** is in force, codified at **Ark. Code § 23-99-1802** via Acts 578 and 597 of 2023, effective August 1, 2023. The federal No Surprises Act explicitly excludes ground ambulance. Arkansas closes that gap.

The weak lever:

- The user-supplied **§ 20-9-302** is **not** the itemization statute. § 20-9-302 governs abortion clinics. The correct itemization cite is **§ 20-9-307**, and the duty is **request-based**, not automatic — the patient must request the itemization to trigger the 30-day delivery duty.

## Hospital itemization right

- **Statute:** **Ark. Code § 20-9-307** — Itemized statement for services, drugs, and supplies
- **Source:** [law.justia.com/codes/arkansas/title-20/subtitle-2/chapter-9/subchapter-3/section-20-9-307](https://law.justia.com/codes/arkansas/title-20/subtitle-2/chapter-9/subchapter-3/section-20-9-307/)
- **What it requires:**
  - Upon **request of the patient** at the time of discharge or at the time of billing, each hospital must furnish to the patient and to the patient's insurance company an itemized listing of all services, drugs, and supplies to be billed.
  - The itemized statement must be furnished **no later than 30 days after discharge**.
  - The hospital must **advise each patient in writing at the time of discharge** of the right to receive the itemized statement.
- **Scope:** Hospitals licensed in Arkansas. The duty runs to both the patient and the insurer.
- **Caveats:**
  - The duty is **not automatic** the way Georgia's O.C.G.A. § 10-1-393(b)(14) is. The patient must affirmatively request the statement. Send a written request, certified mail, to start the 30-day clock and create the paper trail.
  - § 20-9-307 contains **no express private right of action** and no statutory damages for non-compliance. The practical levers when a hospital ignores a request are (a) a consumer complaint to the Arkansas Attorney General Consumer Protection Division, (b) an Arkansas Deceptive Trade Practices Act (ADTPA) claim under § 4-88-113 if the non-compliance is part of a deceptive billing practice, and (c) using the non-compliance as evidence in a collection-defense posture.
- **Heads up on the user-supplied cite:** **§ 20-9-302** is the abortion-clinic provision and has nothing to do with itemized billing. Do not cite § 20-9-302 in a billing dispute letter. The correct cite is § 20-9-307. The user-supplied alternative **§ 23-79-138** does not exist as an itemization provision either; Title 23 Chapter 79 covers insurance policies generally, not hospital billing duties.
- **ERISA:** Not preempted — regulates the provider, not the plan.

## Arkansas Deceptive Trade Practices Act (private right of action)

- **Statute:** **Ark. Code §§ 4-88-101 through 4-88-1404** — Arkansas Deceptive Trade Practices Act (ADTPA). Applicability at **§ 4-88-101**; prohibited practices at **§ 4-88-107** and **§ 4-88-108**; private right of action at **§ 4-88-113(f)**.
- **Source:** [law.justia.com/codes/arkansas/title-4/subtitle-7/chapter-88/subchapter-1/section-4-88-101](https://law.justia.com/codes/arkansas/title-4/subtitle-7/chapter-88/subchapter-1/section-4-88-101/); academic treatment at [law.uark.edu/alr/PDFs/67-2/ArkLRev-67-2-Chaney.pdf](https://law.uark.edu/alr/PDFs/67-2/ArkLRev-67-2-Chaney.pdf)
- **Substance of § 4-88-113(f):** A person who suffers an **actual financial loss** as a result of a violation of the chapter may bring an action to recover **actual damages and reasonable attorney's fees**. Following the 2017 amendments (Act 986), the private remedy requires proof of actual financial loss proximately caused by reliance on the unlawful practice. Treble damages are available to the Attorney General in public enforcement but **not** in the private action.
- **Procedural posture:** No statutory pre-suit demand letter is required for the ADTPA private action, but sending one is strategically sound — it documents the unfair practice, the resulting loss, and the demand for relief, which sharpens the "reliance" and "actual financial loss" elements.
- **Reach over original creditors:** ADTPA generally reaches "any business, sale, lease, or distribution" of goods or services. Application to a hospital's in-house billing department is supported where the billing conduct itself is deceptive — misrepresenting amounts owed, asserting collectability of a time-barred debt (Arkansas's special 2-year SOL makes this risk acute for hospitals), upcoding, billing for services never rendered, misrepresenting in-network status. Cite § 4-88-107 prohibitions explicitly in any 30-day warning letter to an AR provider.
- **Frivolous-action risk:** § 4-88-113(d) and general Arkansas fee-shifting principles allow a prevailing defendant to recover fees in a frivolous action. Frame the deception clearly before pleading.
- **Use:** This is the patient's principal private-action lever against deceptive billing practices in Arkansas, parallel to Georgia's FBPA § 10-1-399 and Alabama's ADTPA § 8-19-10. Lean on it explicitly in any 30-day warning letter to an AR provider.

## Unfair Claims Settlement Practices

- **Statute:** **Ark. Code § 23-66-206** — Unfair methods of competition and unfair or deceptive acts or practices defined; claims-settlement practices listed at **§ 23-66-206(13)**.
- **Source:** [law.justia.com/codes/arkansas/title-23/subtitle-3/chapter-66/subchapter-2/section-23-66-206](https://law.justia.com/codes/arkansas/title-23/subtitle-3/chapter-66/subchapter-2/section-23-66-206/); AID consumer alert at [insurance.arkansas.gov/news/2021/jun/03/consumer-alert-unfair-claims-settlement-practices](https://insurance.arkansas.gov/news/2021/jun/03/consumer-alert-unfair-claims-settlement-practices/)
- **Substance:** Section 23-66-206(13) catalogs the unfair claims-settlement practices an insurer may not commit "with such frequency as to indicate a general business practice" — misrepresenting pertinent facts or policy provisions, failing to acknowledge claim communications promptly, failing to adopt reasonable standards for prompt investigation, refusing to pay claims without reasonable investigation, failing in good faith to effectuate prompt fair settlements where liability is reasonably clear, compelling insureds to litigate, requiring repairs at a particular shop as a condition of payment (§ 23-66-206(13)(O)), and similar conduct.
- **Critical caveat, no private right of action:** Arkansas courts have repeatedly held that **§§ 23-66-202 and 23-66-206 do not create a private cause of action**. Enforcement is by the Arkansas Insurance Commissioner. Patients cannot plead § 23-66-206 as a standalone count in litigation.
- **The alternative private remedy:** **Ark. Code § 23-79-208** — "Damages and attorney's fees on loss claims." Where an insurer "shall fail to pay the losses within the time specified in the policy after demand is made," the policyholder is entitled to recover **12% penalty damages on the amount of the loss, plus reasonable attorney's fees**. Source: [law.justia.com/codes/arkansas/title-23/subtitle-3/chapter-79/subchapter-2/section-23-79-208](https://law.justia.com/codes/arkansas/title-23/subtitle-3/chapter-79/subchapter-2/section-23-79-208/). The 12% penalty is fixed (not capped at "the greater of 50% or $5,000" as in Georgia), and the recovery threshold is generous — the penalty and fees apply if the amount recovered is within **20%** of the amount demanded (30% in homeowner's cases). **This is Arkansas's working statutory pre-judgment lever against a slow-paying carrier and runs in parallel to the common-law Broadway Arms bad-faith tort.**
- **Use:** Cite § 23-66-206 violations in the complaint to the Arkansas Insurance Department (below) and as evidentiary support for both a § 23-79-208 statutory claim and a *Broadway Arms* common-law bad-faith tort. Do not plead § 23-66-206 as a freestanding count.

## Common-law first-party insurance bad faith (Broadway Arms)

- **Authority:** ***Aetna Casualty & Surety Co. v. Broadway Arms Corp.*, 281 Ark. 128, 664 S.W.2d 463 (1984)** — the seminal Arkansas Supreme Court decision recognizing the **tort of bad-faith refusal to pay a first-party insurance claim**. The case grew out of an August 1981 fire loss; the Arkansas Supreme Court decision adopting the tort came down in 1984 (the user's "1981" date refers to the underlying loss; the case citation is the 1984 opinion).
- **Sources:** Case at [law.justia.com/cases/arkansas/supreme-court/1984/83-134-0.html](https://law.justia.com/cases/arkansas/supreme-court/1984/83-134-0.html); doctrinal overview at [chartwelllaw.com/bad-faith-claims-map/arkansas](https://www.chartwelllaw.com/bad-faith-claims-map/arkansas); UALR Law Review case note at [lawrepository.ualr.edu/cgi/viewcontent.cgi?article=1551&context=lawreview](https://lawrepository.ualr.edu/cgi/viewcontent.cgi?httpsredir=1&article=1551&context=lawreview)
- **The standard — "dishonest, malicious, or oppressive":** Bad faith in Arkansas requires **affirmative misconduct that is dishonest, malicious, or oppressive in an attempt to avoid policy liability**. This is a notably high bar — Arkansas is more plaintiff-strict on liability than Oklahoma's *Christian* "no reasonable basis" test, while remaining plaintiff-friendly on damages. The standard expressly excludes:
  - good-faith denials,
  - compromise offers,
  - honest errors in judgment,
  - good-faith controversies over coverage,
  - negligent claim denials (even grossly negligent),
  - delays and administrative confusion.
- **Elements:**
  1. The insured sustained damages.
  2. The insurer engaged in dishonest, malicious, or oppressive conduct in attempting to avoid policy liability.
  3. The bad-faith conduct proximately caused damage to the insured.
- **Damages:**
  - **Compensatory damages** including the policy benefit and consequential losses.
  - **Punitive damages** where evidence shows the defendant "acted wantonly" or with "conscious indifference to the consequences" supporting an inference of malice. The *Broadway Arms* jury awarded **$175,000 compensatory plus $5,000,000 punitive** — a marker that punitive verdicts in Arkansas bad-faith cases can be substantial.
  - **Ark. Code § 16-55-208** caps punitive damages generally at the greater of $250,000 or three times compensatory damages up to $1 million; check for current adjustments and exceptions before relying on uncapped figures.
- **Parallel statutory remedy:** A bad-faith claimant typically pleads **both** the *Broadway Arms* tort and **Ark. Code § 23-79-208** (12% penalty plus attorney's fees). The two are concurrent, not alternative — the statute reaches non-payment within the policy timeframe; the tort reaches dishonest, malicious, or oppressive conduct.
- **Demand mechanics:** No statutory pre-suit demand requirement for the common-law tort itself, but § 23-79-208 is triggered "after demand is made." Send a single written, certified demand identifying the policy, the claim, the amount, and a payment deadline — that one letter satisfies the § 23-79-208 trigger and anchors the *Broadway Arms* record on the "actual knowledge" theory.
- **ERISA preemption:** Common-law bad faith is **preempted** as applied to self-funded ERISA employer plans (*Pilot Life Ins. Co. v. Dedeaux*, 481 U.S. 41 (1987)). Federal remedy is 29 U.S.C. § 1132(a)(1)(B) plus possible § 1132(g) fees, no Arkansas tort recovery. *Broadway Arms* and § 23-79-208 are in play for fully-insured plans, individual/marketplace plans, Medicaid managed care (subject to specific preemption analyses), and Medicare supplements.

## Surprise billing — federal NSA primary, ground ambulance covered by state law

- **State statute:** Arkansas does **not** have a comprehensive standalone surprise-billing act analogous to Georgia's O.C.G.A. § 33-20E-1 et seq. The state instead relies on a federal-state enforcement partnership for the No Surprises Act, with one notable state-law expansion — ground ambulance.
- **Federal floor — No Surprises Act (NSA):** Pub. L. 116-260, Title I of Division BB, effective January 1, 2022. Covers:
  - Emergency services from an out-of-network provider or facility (held to in-network cost-share);
  - Non-emergency services by an OON provider at an in-network facility (anesthesia, radiology, pathology, lab, emergency medicine, neonatology, hospitalist, intensivist, assistant surgeon, diagnostic services);
  - **Air ambulance** (but not ground ambulance — that gap is closed at the state level in Arkansas, see below).
- **Arkansas enforcement:** Arkansas opted into the **state-federal partnership** model — the Arkansas Insurance Department enforces the NSA for state-regulated plans, with the federal CMS retaining backup authority. AID consumer line **1-800-852-5494**; federal No Surprises Help Desk **1-800-985-3059**. Source: [insurance.arkansas.gov/news/2022/jan/11/new-law-protects-consumers-surprise-medical-bills](https://insurance.arkansas.gov/news/2022/jan/11/new-law-protects-consumers-surprise-medical-bills/); ACHI summary at [achi.net/newsroom/arkansas-shares-responsibility-with-federal-government-to-enforce-no-surprises-act](https://achi.net/newsroom/arkansas-shares-responsibility-with-federal-government-to-enforce-no-surprises-act/).
- **ERISA-preempted self-funded plans:** Federal NSA applies; state administrative route is unavailable for the self-funded plan itself, but the patient can route through the federal NSA help desk.

## Ground ambulance balance-billing — Arkansas closes the federal NSA gap

- **Statute:** **Ark. Code § 23-99-1802** — Minimum allowable reimbursement for ground ambulance services; balance-billing prohibition. Added by **Act 597 of 2023** (effective August 1, 2023); related provisions in **Act 578 of 2023** (Transportation Benefit Manager Act). Further amended by **Act 867 of 2025** / **HB 1850 (2025 Reg. Sess.)**, effective **July 1, 2025**.
- **Sources:** [law.justia.com/codes/arkansas/title-23/subtitle-3/chapter-99/subchapter-18/section-23-99-1802](https://law.justia.com/codes/arkansas/title-23/subtitle-3/chapter-99/subchapter-18/section-23-99-1802/); AID Bulletin #5-2024 overview at [ilsainc.com/bulletin/arkansas-issues-advisory-pertaining-to-the-prohibition-of-ground-ambulance-billing](https://www.ilsainc.com/bulletin/arkansas-issues-advisory-pertaining-to-the-prohibition-of-ground-ambulance-billing/); Commonwealth Fund 50-state survey at [commonwealthfund.org/blog/2024/expanding-no-surprises-act-protect-consumers-surprise-ambulance-bills](https://www.commonwealthfund.org/blog/2024/expanding-no-surprises-act-protect-consumers-surprise-ambulance-bills); HB 1850 at [arkleg.state.ar.us/Bills/Detail?id=hb1850&ddBienniumSession=2025/2025R](https://arkleg.state.ar.us/Bills/Detail?id=hb1850&ddBienniumSession=2025/2025R).
- **What it does:**
  - Prohibits balance billing by ground ambulance service providers. The patient's cost-sharing is held to the in-network amount; the patient is held harmless from the rate dispute between the ambulance provider and the insurer.
  - Sets the **minimum allowable reimbursement** the insurer must pay the ambulance provider, based on rates approved or contracted between the ambulance service provider and the local government entity under Ark. Code § 14-266-105. Where no local rate is set, post-July 1, 2025 (per Act 867 of 2025), the minimum is the **lesser of the provider's billed charges or 325% of the Medicare Ambulance Fee Schedule's Arkansas Rural Rate**.
- **Why this matters:** The federal NSA explicitly excludes ground ambulance — Arkansas is one of roughly a dozen states that have closed the gap. For a ground-ambulance bill with a date of service on or after **August 1, 2023**, this is the single highest-leverage Arkansas-specific cite.
- **Caveats:**
  - **ERISA preemption:** § 23-99-1802 does not reach self-funded ERISA employer plans. Those remain subject only to the federal NSA, which excludes ground ambulance — so a self-funded patient in Arkansas may still face a balance bill with no state-law backstop.
  - Excludes air ambulance (federal NSA covers it), workers' compensation, Medicare, and Medicaid.
  - Verify the date of service is on or after **August 1, 2023** (Act 597 effective date) before relying. For dates of service before that, fall back on common-law unconscionability, UCC § 2-305 reasonable-price doctrine, and ADTPA.

## Small claims court — District Court Small Claims Division

- **Court name:** **District Court of [County], Small Claims Division** (Arkansas District Courts hear small claims through a dedicated docket within District Court)
- **Jurisdictional limit:** **$5,000**, codified at **Ark. Code § 16-17-606**
- **Source:** [law.justia.com/codes/arkansas/title-16/subtitle-2/chapter-17/subchapter-6](https://law.justia.com/codes/arkansas/title-16/subtitle-2/chapter-17/subchapter-6/)
- **Filing fees:** typical 2024-2025 range **$30-$65 by county**, plus service fees that often add **~$50** per defendant. Total startup cost generally around **$80-$115**.
- **Attorney rules — the strongest no-lawyer rule in the country:** **Ark. Code § 16-17-606 prohibits attorneys from representing parties in small claims**. Only the natural party in interest may appear; a corporation must appear through a **non-attorney officer or employee**. Arkansas is one of the strictest no-lawyer jurisdictions in the United States. This is a substantial structural advantage for self-represented patients — the hospital cannot bring outside counsel, only a billing-department staffer.
- **Jury trial:** not available in the small claims division. Either party may **appeal de novo within 30 days** to Circuit Court for a jury trial, posting a bond covering the judgment plus costs.
- **Post-judgment interest:** **10% per annum** on judgments (Ark. Code § 16-65-114).

## Statute of limitations — medical-debt-specific 2-year rule

Arkansas is one of a small number of states with a **separate, shorter statute of limitations for medical debt** that beats the general written-contract limit. The 2-year medical-debt SOL is the lead cite in any AR medical-debt collection-defense posture.

- **Medical services (the lead cite):** **2 years** from the date services were performed or from the date of the most recent partial payment, whichever is later — **Ark. Code § 16-56-106**.
  - Applies to actions "to recover charges by a physician or other medical service provider" for services performed after March 31, 1985.
  - Partial payment **restarts** the 2-year clock. **Do not make a partial payment on a time-barred medical debt without legal advice** — a $10 payment on a 22-month-old bill buys the provider a fresh two years.
- **Written contracts (general fallback):** **5 years** from breach — **Ark. Code § 16-56-111**. May apply if the patient signed a separate promissory note or financing agreement that the creditor argues replaces the underlying medical-services contract. The medical-specific 2-year rule should still control where the underlying basis remains "charges for medical services."
- **Oral contracts / open accounts:** **3 years** from breach — **Ark. Code § 16-56-105**.
- **Sources:** § 16-56-106 at [law.justia.com/codes/arkansas/title-16/subtitle-5/chapter-56/subchapter-1/section-16-56-106](https://law.justia.com/codes/arkansas/title-16/subtitle-5/chapter-56/subchapter-1/section-16-56-106/); § 16-56-111 at [law.justia.com/codes/arkansas/title-16/subtitle-5/chapter-56/subchapter-1/section-16-56-111](https://law.justia.com/codes/arkansas/title-16/subtitle-5/chapter-56/subchapter-1/section-16-56-111/); § 16-56-105 at [law.justia.com/codes/arkansas/title-16/subtitle-5/chapter-56/subchapter-1/section-16-56-105](https://law.justia.com/codes/arkansas/title-16/subtitle-5/chapter-56/subchapter-1/section-16-56-105/)

**Practical posture:** Always plead § 16-56-106 first. If the bill is over 2 years old from the date of service (and no partial payment has restarted the clock), the collection action is time-barred — but the defense is **affirmative** and must be raised in the answer. The kit should foreground this in any AR-bound dispute letter or 30-day warning to a collector.

## Hospital lien statute

- **Citations:** **Ark. Code §§ 18-46-101 through 18-46-117** — Medical, Nursing, Hospital, and Ambulance Service Lien Act
- **Sources:** § 18-46-101 (title) at [law.justia.com/codes/arkansas/2020/title-18/subtitle-4/chapter-46/section-18-46-101](https://law.justia.com/codes/arkansas/2020/title-18/subtitle-4/chapter-46/section-18-46-101/); § 18-46-105 (notice requirements) at [law.justia.com/codes/arkansas/title-18/subtitle-4/chapter-46/section-18-46-105](https://law.justia.com/codes/arkansas/title-18/subtitle-4/chapter-46/section-18-46-105/); § 18-46-106 at [codes.findlaw.com/ar/title-18-property/ar-code-sect-18-46-106](https://codes.findlaw.com/ar/title-18-property/ar-code-sect-18-46-106.html)
- **Substance:** Medical practitioners, nurses, hospitals, **and ambulance service providers** have a lien for reasonable charges for services to a person injured by another's tort, **only against the patient's cause of action and any recovery from a third party** liable for the injury (the at-fault driver, the at-fault party's insurer). **Not a lien on the patient's home, wages, or general bank accounts.**
- **Perfection requirements (§ 18-46-105):**
  - Serve and file a notice of claim of lien. Perfection is complete once notice is served and filed; no additional steps are required.
  - The notice must include:
    - Name and address of the tortfeasor (and, if a lien is claimed against an insurer, the insurer's name and address);
    - Name of the patient, usual address, and whereabouts when notice is served if different;
    - Name and address of the lienholder, with the lienholder's category (practitioner, nurse, hospital, or ambulance service);
    - Time, place, and circumstances of the alleged fault or neglect, and nature of the injury;
    - Amount claimed, if service has been completed.
  - Notice may be served and recorded at any time while service is being rendered, or after discontinuance of service, so long as the underlying claim is not barred by the statute of limitations.
- **Key patient-side leverage points:**
  - The lien attaches only to a tort recovery — patients with no liable third-party tortfeasor face no lien exposure under Chapter 46.
  - Defective notice (missing tortfeasor address, missing patient whereabouts, missing service circumstances) defeats the lien. Always inspect the notice for completeness.
  - The Medical, Nursing, Hospital, and Ambulance Service Lien Act expressly covers **ambulance service providers** as well — relevant where an ambulance bill is the disputed charge in a tort posture.

## Hospital charity care

Arkansas has **no state statute** requiring a comprehensive hospital financial-assistance policy beyond federal law. Non-profit hospitals are bound by **IRS § 501(r)** (federal): required Financial Assistance Policy, required Community Health Needs Assessment, limits on extraordinary collection actions, and capping amounts charged to FAP-eligible patients at amounts generally billed to insured patients. § 501(r) is the binding floor; Arkansas does not exceed it.

For-profit hospitals and county/municipal hospitals have no state FAP mandate. The **Arkansas Volunteer Health Care Act** ([healthy.arkansas.gov/programs-services/prevention-healthy-living/rural-health-primary-care/charitable-clinics-grant-program/arkansas-volunteer-health-care-act](https://healthy.arkansas.gov/programs-services/prevention-healthy-living/rural-health-primary-care/charitable-clinics-grant-program/arkansas-volunteer-health-care-act/)) encourages volunteer charitable clinics but does not impose a financial-assistance duty on hospitals. The **Medical Quota Act** (Ark. Code § 6-64-503) defines "medically indigent" but is tied to specific state programs, not a general hospital duty.

Practical posture: For non-profit AR hospitals, demand the § 501(r) FAP in writing, screen the patient against the published FAP eligibility thresholds, and reference the federal FAP duty rather than any AR-specific statute (which does not exist).

Use Dollar For at [dollarfor.org/state_sheet/arkansas](https://dollarfor.org/state_sheet/arkansas/) and Community Catalyst's Arkansas page at [communitycatalyst.org/initiatives-and-issues/initiatives/hospital-accountability-project/free-care/states/arkansas](https://www.communitycatalyst.org/initiatives-and-issues/initiatives/hospital-accountability-project/free-care/states/arkansas) for screening.

## Credit reporting

Arkansas has **not** enacted a state-specific medical-debt credit-reporting restriction. Patients in Arkansas rely on:

- The 2022-2023 voluntary changes by Equifax/Experian/TransUnion (paid medical debt removed; debt under $500 not reported; 1-year delay before reporting).
- Federal FCRA dispute rights (15 U.S.C. §§ 1681i, 1681s-2).

**Federal preemption posture is in flux.** The CFPB issued an October 2025 interpretive rule taking the position that the FCRA preempts state laws restricting medical-debt credit reporting. If that survives challenge, any future Arkansas state-level law would face the same preemption analysis.

For deceptive furnishing of medical-debt information to credit bureaus, ADTPA § 4-88-107 may apply where the furnishing is itself deceptive (e.g., reporting a time-barred debt under § 16-56-106 as currently collectable), and the patient may have a federal FCRA claim against the furnisher.

## Wage garnishment

- **Statute:** Arkansas follows the federal **Consumer Credit Protection Act** garnishment cap; general post-judgment garnishment procedure at **Ark. Code §§ 16-110-401 through 16-110-417**.
- **Substance:** Medical-bill creditors cannot garnish wages without first obtaining a court judgment. Post-judgment, garnishment is capped at the federal CCPA level — **25% of disposable earnings**, or the amount by which weekly disposable earnings exceed 30× the federal minimum wage, whichever is less.
- **Arkansas exemption:** Arkansas constitutional and statutory homestead and personal-property exemptions (Ark. Const. art. 9 §§ 1-2; Ark. Code § 16-66-218) are unusually patient-friendly relative to neighboring states. Confirm with current counsel before relying.
- **Use:** In response letters to collectors threatening garnishment without a judgment in hand — and to flag the structural barrier where the underlying debt may already be time-barred under § 16-56-106.

## Key Arkansas-specific advantages

Worth keeping in mind when triaging an AR patient's bills:

1. **2-year medical-debt SOL (§ 16-56-106)** — Arkansas's medical-specific limitations period is shorter than the general 5-year written-contract limit, and shorter than most neighboring states. For any bill more than 2 years old from the date of service (with no partial-payment restart), the collection action is time-barred. Lead with this in every AR collection-defense posture.
2. **No attorneys in small claims (§ 16-17-606)** — Arkansas is one of the strictest no-lawyer small-claims jurisdictions in the country. Corporate defendants must appear through a non-attorney officer or employee. This compresses the hospital's defense economics and shifts leverage decisively toward self-represented patients. Pair the SOL defense with a small-claims posture for maximum effect.
3. **Ground-ambulance balance-billing protection (§ 23-99-1802, effective Aug 1, 2023)** — Arkansas closes the federal NSA's biggest gap. Always check whether a ground-ambulance bill post-dates August 1, 2023. Verify the patient's plan is state-regulated (not ERISA self-funded) before relying.
4. **12% statutory penalty plus attorney's fees on insurance loss claims (§ 23-79-208)** — a robust statutory pre-judgment lever that runs in parallel to the common-law *Broadway Arms* bad-faith tort. Triggered by a written demand and non-payment within the policy's specified time. The 20% recovery-tolerance (30% for homeowners) is generous.
5. **ADTPA reach over original creditors (§ 4-88-101 et seq.)** — reaches the hospital itself, not just third-party collectors, where billing conduct is itself deceptive. Attorney's fees are recoverable in the private action under § 4-88-113(f).
6. **Hospital lien is third-party-recovery-only (§ 18-46-101 et seq.)** — no lien on home, wages, or general bank accounts. Defective notice defeats the lien; always inspect for completeness.

## What Arkansas does **not** have (vs. Georgia)

For accurate triage, the lever-gaps matter as much as the levers:

- **No automatic itemization duty.** Arkansas is request-based under § 20-9-307; the six-business-day Georgia rule has no AR analog.
- **No private right of action under the Unfair Trade Practices Act.** § 23-66-206 is regulator-only; the patient's working private remedy is § 23-79-208 (12% penalty plus fees) and the *Broadway Arms* tort.
- **No comprehensive state surprise-billing act** like Georgia's O.C.G.A. § 33-20E-1. Arkansas relies on the federal NSA via a state-federal partnership, with the ground-ambulance gap closed at the state level by § 23-99-1802 and otherwise nothing comprehensive.
- **No state medical-debt credit-reporting law.**
- **No state hospital charity-care mandate** beyond federal § 501(r) for non-profit hospitals.
- **Smaller small-claims limit, $5,000 vs. Georgia's $15,000** — many medical-bill disputes will exceed the AR cap and require Circuit Court (where attorneys are permitted). The no-attorney advantage applies only up to $5,000.
- **No statutory § 33-4-6-style structured bad-faith demand.** Arkansas's bad-faith remedy is common-law (*Broadway Arms*) plus the parallel statutory penalty at § 23-79-208 — no fixed 60-day-demand structure.
