# Common dispute outcomes

A reference for calibrating expectations. Public reporting and patient-advocacy data give us a rough sense of what patients can expect from medical-bill disputes — settlement rates, reduction amounts, time-to-resolution. None of these are guarantees, but they help patients decide whether a fight is worth picking.

Note: this document compiles publicly reported figures from advocacy organizations, journalism, and government statistics. It does not contain proprietary data. Patterns shift over time; verify recent figures before relying.

## Bill itemization requests

**Typical outcome:** The hospital provides an itemized bill within the state-statutory window (usually 30 days), no reduction at this stage.

**What happens next:** The itemized bill is the foundation for everything else. Patients who request itemization and then identify a specific issue (upcoding, services not received, duplicate charges) tend to recover 20-50% reductions on disputed lines.

**What patients miss:** A surprising fraction of patients receiving an itemized bill simply accept it without scrutiny. Itemization is necessary but not sufficient; the dispute on specific lines is where the reduction happens.

## Initial dispute letters

**Typical outcome (well-grounded dispute):** 30-60% reduction on the disputed amount, or a settlement offer of 50-70 cents on the dollar for the disputed portion.

**What helps:**

- Specific evidence (screenshot of fair-market price, citation to CPT documentation requirements)
- Certified mail (creates the paper trail)
- A specific dollar offer rather than a vague "this is too much"
- Tone that's firm but not adversarial

**What hurts:**

- Generic complaints without specifics
- Threats early in the process
- Asking for too much (e.g., complete write-off when the bill has only a single coding issue)

## No Surprises Act balance-billing disputes

**Typical outcome:** Full reversal of the balance bill, often within 30-90 days. NSA cases are the highest-leverage dispute category in the federal system because the law is clear and CMS enforces.

**What helps:**

- Specific identification of the scenario (emergency, OON ancillary at in-network facility, air ambulance)
- Parallel CMS complaint at 1-800-985-3059
- Insurance company involvement (they have a stake too)

**What hurts:**

- Ground ambulance — federal NSA does not cover. State-by-state.

## Insurance denial appeals

**Typical outcome at internal appeal:** Around 40-50% of internal appeals are reversed in the patient's favor. The remaining ~50% proceed to external review.

**Typical outcome at external review (IRO):** Around 40-60% of external reviews are reversed in the patient's favor. Total across both stages: roughly 60-70% of well-grounded denial appeals are eventually reversed.

**What helps:**

- Treating physician's medical-necessity letter
- Citations to clinical practice guidelines and peer-reviewed evidence
- Identifying the specific plan provision that obligates coverage
- Parallel state DOI complaint (especially for fully-insured plans)
- For ERISA self-funded: DOL EBSA informal intervention at 1-866-444-3272

## IRS § 501(r) Financial Assistance applications

**Typical outcome:** Patients meeting the hospital's FAP eligibility criteria receive significant or complete write-offs. Eligibility is typically pegged to a multiple of the federal poverty level (200%-400% FPL).

**What helps:**

- Run [Dollar For](https://dollarfor.org)'s screener first — they handle the application for free and have negotiated relationships with many hospital systems.
- Apply early — before extraordinary collection actions begin.
- Be honest about income and assets — providing false information can be ground for denial.

**Real-world figures:**

- Dollar For has facilitated $136M+ in debt write-offs.
- Estimated $14 billion in charity care goes unclaimed annually because eligible patients don't apply.

## FDCPA § 1692g debt validation requests

**Typical outcome (within 30-day window):** Around 30-50% of disputed third-party medical debts simply disappear — the collector cannot produce the documentation FDCPA requires (original contract, full itemization, chain of assignment) and the file is closed.

**What helps:**

- Filing within 30 days of first written contact
- Certified-mail tracking proof
- Specific requests (not a generic "verify this debt")

**What patients miss:**

- This only works against **third-party** collectors, not the original hospital or doctor's office.

## Small claims court (where the patient files)

**Typical outcome:** Most corporate defendants settle before the hearing date. Of those that go to hearing, prepared self-represented plaintiffs prevail in 60-80% of cases on well-grounded medical-billing disputes.

**What helps:**

- Documented escalation ladder (initial dispute, 30-day warning, then filing)
- Preparation (mock-walkthrough, exhibits labeled, statement of claim under 200 words)
- Showing up on time
- Sticking to facts, not emotion

**What hurts:**

- Filing in the wrong venue
- Naming the wrong defendant (use the registered legal name)
- Missing the statute of limitations
- Going alone against a case that needs an attorney (over $10k, complex legal issues)

## Hardship reductions on correctly-billed but unaffordable charges

**Typical outcome (non-profit hospital):** 50-100% reduction via § 501(r) FAP; payment plan with no interest for the remainder. Most non-profits will reduce significantly when asked properly.

**Typical outcome (for-profit hospital or other provider):** 30-50% reduction with hardship documentation; payment plan at low interest.

**What helps:**

- Specific dollar offer anchored to Medicare allowable rate
- Documentation of household income relative to federal poverty level
- Refusal to put a credit card on file or auto-debit
- Demanding consideration of FAP for non-profit hospitals

## Bankruptcy (Chapter 7)

**Typical outcome:** Medical debt is fully discharged within 3-4 months of filing. Credit-report mark for 10 years.

**What patients miss:**

- Disputing the underlying bills first can eliminate the debt without the credit-report impact.
- Filing fee ($338) plus attorney fees ($1,000-$2,500 for routine Chapter 7); fee waiver available below 150% FPL.

## Time-to-resolution by track

Rough averages:

- Itemization request: 30-45 days from mailing
- Initial dispute response: 2-4 weeks for substantive response
- 30-day warning to settlement: 6-12 weeks total from initial dispute
- Small claims filing to judgment: 60-120 days from filing
- ERISA internal appeal: 30-60 days
- ERISA external review: 45-60 days after internal appeal denial
- Total ERISA appeal path: 3-6 months
- Medicare 5-level appeal: 6-24 months depending on level reached
- Medicaid two-step appeal: 60-120 days
- HHS OCR complaint: 6-12 months
- CMS Hospital Price Transparency complaint: investigation can take 6-18 months
- IRS § 501(r) FAP application: 30-90 days for hospital determination
- Bankruptcy Chapter 7: 3-4 months from filing to discharge

## What this all adds up to

Most patients who dispute a medical bill recover something. The fraction who recover everything is smaller but meaningful. The fraction who recover nothing is small — usually because the underlying bill was actually correct and the patient didn't realize the leverage was in negotiation rather than dispute.

The strongest predictor of a successful dispute is the patient's willingness to engage in the paper trail: certified-mail letters, exhibits, evidence, escalation. The system bets on patient fatigue. Pushing through fatigue is the actual work.

## Caveat

These figures are rough averages from public reporting. Your individual case may differ. The kit's purpose is to maximize the probability and magnitude of a favorable outcome given the facts of your specific dispute, not to guarantee any specific result.
