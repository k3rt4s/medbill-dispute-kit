# Template — Subrogation response

Use when the patient's health insurance plan has asserted a subrogation or reimbursement claim against a tort recovery the patient is pursuing (motor-vehicle, slip-and-fall, premises liability, product liability). The plan typically sends a "subrogation questionnaire" or a "letter of representation" to the patient, claiming the right to be reimbursed out of any settlement.

The legal landscape is split. ERISA self-funded plans with proper plan-document language usually have strong subrogation rights under Sereboff v. Mid Atlantic Medical Services, 547 U.S. 356 (2006) and US Airways v. McCutchen, 569 U.S. 88 (2013). Fully-insured plans and government plans (Medicare, Medicaid, TRICARE) operate under different rules, with state anti-subrogation statutes potentially in play. In every case, the plan's claim is limited by the plan-document language — most plans cannot recover until the patient has been made whole, and most cannot recover from the patient personally beyond the recovery itself.

This template (a) demands the plan identify its claim in writing with the plan provisions it relies on, (b) preserves the made-whole defense, (c) reduces the claim by the common-fund (attorney's-fees) doctrine, and (d) opens the door to negotiation.

Use only after the dispute over the underlying medical bills is settled, or in parallel if the bill and subrogation tracks are intertwined.

To Subrogation:

I am the member identified above. I have received notice that [INSURER NAME] (or [RECOVERY VENDOR] acting on the plan's behalf) intends to assert a subrogation or reimbursement claim against any recovery I may obtain from [AT-FAULT PARTY / LIABILITY INSURER] in connection with the incident referenced above. This letter responds to that notice and sets out my position.

I. Information requested from the plan before any further action

Before I respond substantively to any reimbursement amount, please provide the following in writing within thirty (30) days, pursuant to my right of access to plan documents under ERISA § 104(b)(4) / 29 U.S.C. § 1024(b)(4) (if the plan is ERISA-governed) or the equivalent state insurance disclosure rule:

1. A copy of the plan's Summary Plan Description (SPD) and the master plan document, including specifically the subrogation, reimbursement, equitable lien, and any "made-whole" or "common-fund" override provisions.

2. Confirmation of the plan's funding status: is the plan self-funded (and therefore ERISA-governed) or fully insured? If fully insured, identify the underwriting insurance company and the state of insurance.

3. The total dollar amount the plan asserts as its subrogation interest, broken down by claim number, date of service, provider, allowed amount, plan payment, and patient cost-sharing.

4. The plan's position on the made-whole doctrine: does the plan-document language expressly disclaim the made-whole doctrine, or is the doctrine preserved? Cite the specific section.

5. The plan's position on the common-fund doctrine and attorney's fees: does the plan-document language expressly disclaim the common-fund reduction, or is the doctrine preserved? Cite the specific section.

6. If the recovery vendor is a third party (not the insurer or plan administrator directly), the contractual basis for the vendor's authority to act on behalf of the plan.

7. Confirmation of whether any state anti-subrogation statute applies (e.g., for fully insured plans in [PATIENT STATE], whether [STATE STATUTE] restricts subrogation for personal-injury claims).

II. Position pending receipt of plan documents

Until I receive and review the plan documents, my position is as follows:

[The drafter renders the positions that apply, drawing from the available context.]

[POSITION A — Made-whole doctrine]

I have not been and will not be made whole by any reasonably expected recovery in this matter. My damages include economic loss (the unreimbursed medical bills and out-of-pocket expense of $[OOP AMOUNT]), lost earnings of $[LOST EARNINGS], future medical care reasonably expected to cost $[FUTURE CARE], and non-economic damages including pain and suffering valued at no less than $[PAIN AND SUFFERING]. The total is approximately $[TOTAL DAMAGES]. Any recovery substantially below that total fails to make me whole. Unless the plan-document language expressly disclaims the made-whole doctrine, the plan's reimbursement claim does not attach until I have been made whole, which I have not been.

[POSITION B — Common-fund / attorney's-fees reduction]

The plan's recovery, if any, is subject to a pro rata reduction for the attorney's fees and litigation costs that produced the recovery. The fee in my case is [N]% pursuant to a written contingency agreement. After attorney's fees and litigation costs, my net recovery before any subrogation reduction is $[NET RECOVERY]. The plan's pro rata share of fees and costs is $[PRO RATA FEES]; any subrogation reimbursement must be reduced by that amount before payment.

[POSITION C — Allocation between economic and non-economic damages]

In any settlement, the parties intend to allocate the proceeds between economic damages (medical, lost wages) and non-economic damages (pain and suffering, loss of consortium). Subrogation reaches only the economic component, not the non-economic component. The allocation of [ECONOMIC %] economic and [NON-ECONOMIC %] non-economic is fair given the nature of the injury, the duration of recovery, and comparable verdicts. The plan's recoverable interest is limited to the economic portion.

[POSITION D — No Surprises Act amounts are not subrogation-eligible]

To the extent any portion of the medical claims giving rise to the plan's reimbursement claim were emergency or out-of-network ancillary services governed by the federal No Surprises Act, the plan's payment for those claims was a contractual obligation under the plan's compliance with 42 U.S.C. § 300gg-111, not an injury-related expenditure subject to subrogation. The amount adjudicated under the NSA in-network cost-sharing rule cannot be subrogated against a tort recovery as if it had been a discretionary plan expenditure.

[POSITION E — Disputed billing not yet resolved]

A portion of the medical bills the plan paid is still in active dispute with the provider for amounts above the EOB-allowed amount. To the extent that disputed amounts are reduced, the plan's reimbursement basis is correspondingly reduced. The plan should not press a subrogation claim on amounts the provider has not yet been finally determined to be entitled to.

III. Negotiating posture

I am willing to discuss a reasonable resolution once the plan documents above are received and reviewed. My preferred resolution sequence is:

1. Plan provides documents and dollar breakdown.

2. The made-whole, common-fund, and allocation positions above are reflected in any proposed subrogation amount.

3. The plan and I (or my counsel) agree on a final subrogation figure in writing, with the plan acknowledging full satisfaction of its claim upon payment.

4. The plan provides a written release of any further subrogation, reimbursement, or equitable-lien interest in the recovery.

IV. Notice of representation (render if applicable)

I am represented by counsel in the underlying tort matter: [ATTORNEY NAME], [LAW FIRM], [ATTORNEY ADDRESS], [ATTORNEY PHONE]. Please direct any further substantive communication to counsel; this letter is sent by me directly to ensure the plan has prompt notice of the positions above before any settlement is reduced to writing.

V. Privilege and offer of compromise

This letter is sent under [STATE] Rule of Evidence 408 (or its federal or state equivalent) as an offer of compromise. No statement is an admission of liability for any amount.

Please confirm receipt and direct your response to the address above.

## Placeholders and rendering notes

- `[INSURER NAME]` vs. `[RECOVERY VENDOR NAME]` — many plans contract subrogation out to vendors (Optum, Equian/Optum Recovery, The Rawlings Group, Trover Solutions). The vendor letterhead is often what the patient sees. The letter is sent to the vendor with a CC to the plan administrator on file.
- `[STATE STATUTE]` — state anti-subrogation rules vary widely. The drafter loads `references/laws_state_<code>.md` for any subrogation entry. Some states (e.g., VA for fully-insured plans, KS for certain plan types) limit subrogation more than others.
- ERISA preempts most state law for self-funded plans; the made-whole and common-fund defenses then turn entirely on the plan-document language. That is why item I.1 (the plan-document request) is at the top of the letter.

## Before sending

The drafter confirms:

1. The patient actually has a tort recovery pending or in negotiation. If there is no tort claim, there is no subrogation interest; the right tool is the regular dispute letter.
2. The patient is not represented by an attorney for the tort matter, OR the attorney has approved the letter going out under the patient's name. Patient-direct communication during attorney representation is permitted but should be coordinated.
3. The medical bills underlying the subrogation are themselves settled or otherwise quantified. Sending this letter while the bills are still in active dispute is premature; the disputed amount is moving.

## Parallel actions

- If the plan refuses to provide plan documents within 30 days: file an ERISA § 502(c) penalty claim. Plan administrators face a per-day penalty (currently up to $110/day) for failing to respond to a written request for plan documents.
- If the recovery vendor continues to communicate with the at-fault party's insurer attempting to perfect a direct claim against the recovery (rather than through the plan member), notify both the plan administrator and the at-fault party's insurer that the vendor is not authorized to receive funds directly.

## Follow-up

- 30 days from postmark, no plan documents → ERISA § 502(c) escalation.
- Once documents are received: review made-whole and common-fund language and respond with a specific dollar counter-position.
- When the tort matter approaches settlement: do not sign any release that includes a subrogation-payment provision until the plan's subrogation claim is finalized in writing. Subrogation paid out of settlement proceeds, on a separate written agreement with the plan, is the standard pattern.
